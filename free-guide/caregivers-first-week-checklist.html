The Caregiver's First-Week Checklist

When you suddenly become someone's caregiver, the to-do list is endless and the fog is real. This is not everything — it's just enough to get through the first week without dropping what matters most. Do what you can. Cross off the rest with a clear conscience.

Right away

  • Write down the diagnosis, the main doctor's name, and one phone number that reaches a real person.
  • Make a single list of every medication, dose, and time — keep it on the fridge and on your phone.
  • Find the insurance card and note the member ID somewhere safe.
  • Ask the care team: "Who do I call after hours, and when should I worry?"
  • Tell two people what's happening. You should not be the only one who knows.

This week

  • Set up one shared calendar for appointments (paper or phone — whatever you'll actually use).
  • Start a simple notebook: questions for the doctor, symptoms, what changed today.
  • Ask whether a home-health visit, social worker, or case manager is available — and request one.
  • Identify one task you can hand to someone else, and actually hand it over.
  • Check whether the pharmacy can sync refills and deliver.
  • Locate important documents (ID, advance directive, power of attorney) — even if you don't act on them yet.

For your own footing

  • Eat something and drink water. You can't pour from an empty cup — and you know it.
  • Pick one person to be your "I'm not okay" call, and tell them they're it.
  • Decide what can wait. Most of it can.
  • Look up one respite or support option near you (start with the resource library).
  • Save a crisis number now, before you need it: in the US, call or text 988.

Questions worth asking the care team

  • What is the goal of this treatment — and what should we expect?
  • What changes mean "call us now" versus "mention it next visit"?
  • What help exists that we haven't asked about — home care, equipment, financial aid?
  • If things get harder, who guides us on the next decisions?
A reminder for the hard days: you will not do this perfectly, and that is not the same as doing it wrong. Showing up is the work. — The Cardinal's Toolkit
