Quick Start Guide

Something changed and your head is spinning. Answer these four questions, in order. That’s the whole job right now.

1. What is happening, in one sentence?

2. Is anyone unsafe today? (If yes — call the doctor or 911 first. The rest of this page can wait.)

3. Who is the next phone call?

4. What are the first three steps — small ones count?

You don’t have to do this perfectly. You have to do the next thing.

Action Checklists

Three of the moments that start the caregiving road. Check what’s done. Circle what’s stuck.

After a new diagnosis

  • Ask the doctor: What does this mean for daily life in the next 6 months?
  • Get a copy of the visit summary before you leave
  • Start the Medication List (page in this toolbox)
  • Ask who coordinates care — get a name and a direct number
  • Tell one family member today; schedule a family meeting this week

Before a hospital discharge

  • Ask for the discharge plan in writing — don’t leave without it
  • Confirm every medication change against your list
  • Ask: What would send us back to the ER, and what do we do instead?
  • Ask about home health, therapy, or equipment orders — who arranges them, and when
  • Book the follow-up appointment before you leave the building

New to caregiving

  • Locate the important papers: insurance cards, ID, medication bottles
  • Ask about (or start) a durable power of attorney and health care proxy
  • Fill in Important Contacts (page in this toolbox)
  • Pick one person who can relieve you for two hours a week
  • Write down what Mom or Dad says they want — in their words

Hospital Bag List

ER trips don’t make appointments. A packed bag turns a panicked scramble into picking something up on the way out.

For your parent

  • Photo ID & insurance cards (copies)
  • Medication List (from this toolbox)
  • Copy of POA / health care proxy / DNR if one exists
  • Glasses, hearing aids & batteries, dentures case
  • Comfortable change of clothes & slip-proof socks
  • Sweater (hospitals are cold)
  • Toothbrush & toiletries
  • Phone charger (long cable)
  • A comfort item — photo, small blanket

For you, the caregiver

  • Your own charger & snacks
  • Notebook & pen — write down every name and every instruction
  • Important Contacts page (from this toolbox)
  • Water bottle
  • Any of your own daily medications
  • Something to read — waiting is the job

Facility Tour Checklist

Be an informed buyer. The lobby is staged; lunch on a Tuesday is not. Trust your eyes and your nose, and write it down before you drive away.

Facility name / date of tour:

Ask them

  • What’s the staff-to-resident ratio — days, nights, and weekends?
  • What exactly is included in the base rate, and what costs extra?
  • What happens when care needs increase? What would make you ask us to leave?
  • How do you communicate with families — and how fast after an incident?
  • May I see the most recent state inspection report?

Watch for yourself

  • Do staff greet residents by name?
  • Are residents out of their rooms, engaged — or parked in hallways?
  • How does it smell — honestly?
  • Would Mom or Dad actually eat this food?

Hospice Questions

Hospice is not giving up. It is choosing comfort, presence, and support — for the person and for you. These questions help you choose well.

Ask the hospice team

  • What does hospice actually provide — visits, equipment, medications, and how often?
  • Who do we call at 2 a.m., and who shows up?
  • What support is there for the family — counseling, respite, chaplain, bereavement?
  • What will you help us understand about what’s coming, so nothing surprises us?
  • What does Medicare cover, and is there anything we would ever be billed for?
  • If things improve, can we pause or leave hospice? (Yes — ask them to explain how.)

Ask each other

What matters most to Mom or Dad now — comfort, home, people, quiet?

What do we want to make sure gets said, while it can still be said?
