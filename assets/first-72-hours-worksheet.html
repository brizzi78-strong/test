The First 72 Hours

When everything just changed — a diagnosis, a fall, a phone call — this is the next step. One page a day. That's the whole job.

You don't have to do this perfectly. You have to do the next thing.

This worksheet is three days of next things. Don't work ahead. Don't read it all tonight. Do today's page, then stop and rest — resting is on the list because it's part of the job.

The Cardinal's Toolbox

Day 1 · The first 24 hours

Steady the ground

Before anything else: if anyone is unsafe right now, call the doctor or 911 first. This page will still be here.

What happened, in one sentence?

Plain words. Not the medical term — what you'd tell your best friend.

What exactly were you told, and by whom?

Name and role of who said it. If it was a doctor, ask the office for the visit summary — you're entitled to it.

The one question you wish you had asked:

Call the office tomorrow and ask it. Doctors expect the call-back. Write their answer here.

Before you sleep tonight

  • Tell one person. Just one. Say it out loud to somebody.
  • Eat something. Drink water.
  • Put every medication bottle in one bag or basket — don't organize it yet, just gather it.
  • Stop researching by 9 p.m. The forty open tabs will not help you tonight.

Day 1 is not for decisions. It's for standing up.

Day 3 · The next right steps

Point the road forward

You're not solving everything today. You're setting three things in motion and saying one thing out loud.

  • Book the follow-up appointment (date: )
  • Schedule a family meeting this week — everyone, in person or on the phone (date: )
  • Pick one person who can relieve you for two hours a week (name: )

At the family meeting, ask these five questions

What changed?

What are we worried about?

What does Mom/Dad want most?

Ask them. Write it in their words, not yours.

What decision needs to be made now?

Who is responsible for the next step?

Disagreement at the meeting is allowed. Leaving without a next step is not.

After the 72 hours

The road ahead has names on it — Medicare, Medicaid, dementia, assisted living, hospice — and every one of them is more manageable met prepared than met at 2 a.m. in a discharge office.
