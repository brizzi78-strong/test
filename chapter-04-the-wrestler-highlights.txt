CHAPTER 4: The Wrestler

I was twelve when Uncle Charlie suggested I try wrestling. It wasn't an assessment of talent — I had no observable talent. I was awkward, undersized, the kind of kid who got picked somewhere in the forgettable middle of gym class. Charlie just had a hunch, the way people sometimes redirect a kid toward something because nothing else has stuck yet.

That one suggestion rearranged the entire direction of my life. Without Charlie there is no wrestling room, no county championship at seventeen, no mat teaching me how to stay when everything in me wanted to leave, no foundation underneath me years later when the floor gave out. One sentence, and everything changed. Lou said what Lou said about most things: we'll try it. The quiet confidence of a man who believed that most problems yield to sustained effort and the only way to find out is to show up. So I showed up.

The wrestling room was always warmer than the rest of the building — years of work soaked into the walls. I loved that room, not because it was comfortable but because it was the one place I didn't have to figure out who I was supposed to be. I just had to work, and work was something I understood. Wrestling is the loneliest sport there is. You step onto the mat and nobody can help you. No substitutions, no timeouts that matter. Just you and him and six minutes and whatever you brought with you that week. Your body tells the truth on a wrestling mat whether you want it to or not. In classrooms, at parties, I could perform — assemble the version of myself that seemed right for the room. On the mat there was no version. There was just what I could do and what I couldn't, and I found out which was which in real time. That was the part I needed.

It did not start well. My first match in sixth grade, the kid shot a double-leg I saw coming and couldn't stop, put me on my back, and pinned me before I understood what had happened. I lay there looking at the gym ceiling and felt something flat and final — the discovery that you are further from where you want to be than you thought. My first varsity match lasted nineteen seconds. I finished my freshman year 2 and 4.

And through all of it, Lou was in the stands. Third row, usually. Arms crossed — not in disapproval but in attention. He never missed a match in four years. Not the nineteen-second loss, not the close ones that went the wrong way, not the good ones when the work finally started showing up. My mother and Aunt Nancey were there too, every match — the woman who had carried Chris and me across a border on nothing but courage, in the stands, every single time.

The turn came early sophomore year. I drew the top seed in a tournament — a kid everyone knew would win, a kid I was scheduled to be a warm-up for. Something happened in that match I still can't fully explain. The fear that had been the ground note of every match since that first pin just wasn't there. What was there instead was anger — not at him, but at the idea that I was supposed to lose. I beat him. Not cleanly. A grinding, ugly, three-period match. But I won it. After, Lou didn't say much. A hand on my shoulder. Something that translated, in the language he used when words were insufficient, to: I always knew this was in you. I won the District championship at 119 that year. My name went up on the wall in the gym. 1994. The first of my name on that wall.

Junior year I cut nineteen pounds the week of the tournament, made weight, then binged so much food that I threw up twice on the mat during a match against a guy who'd beaten me twice. The ref stopped it. Greeny grabbed a towel, cleaned me up, sent me back out. I won that match and the tournament. Then I drew Dan Pires in Regions — a name in Bergen County wrestling, seeded second to my seventh. Six minutes. Ten seconds left, I'm down 5-4, and he lets me up, confident he can run out the clock on his feet. He should not have let me up. Three seconds left, I shoot a single-leg. My arms found his leg before my brain caught up. Takedown. 6-5. Final. The headline the next morning read: "Late Takedown Keeps Ramsey's Brizzi Going." Lou sat with me afterward while the crowd moved around us and the bracket continued without us. Then, finally: "That's who you are," he said. Not great match. Not I knew you could do it. That's who you are. I just nodded. That was enough for him.

Senior year I came into the Counties as the top seed at 130. My brother Chris was a freshman at 103. In the sauna we'd joked it would be cool to share an article someday. Then Chris upset the number one seed to make the semis, and the paper ran it: "Ramsey's Brizzi Brothers are Double Trouble." Dad was beaming. I won the semis, won the final 15-7, and took the county championship at seventeen years old. My name went on that wall again. I would not understand the significance of that number until January 9, 2026 — seventeen years into my sobriety — when a red cardinal landed on my car mirror and the symmetry hit me like something that had been there all along.

I finished high school 81 and 18. From 2-4 as a freshman to a school record as a senior. From nineteen seconds to county champion.

Chris went on to rewrite every record I set — 120-4, three county titles, second at freestyle nationals in Fargo. The kid the school system once wanted to hold back in third grade. Nothing but pride. Lou built us both, just differently, and he never compared us. He showed up for Chris's records and my county title with identical, uncomplicated pride. He didn't care who stood higher on the podium. He cared that we stood.

I learned something about people that year, too. My coach Albersi was around when I was winning and scarce when I was struggling — leaders want to be associated with winners. That's how the world works. But Lou was the exception to every version of that rule. Through the nineteen seconds, the backup year, the weight cutting, the throwing up, the losses that hurt more than the wins — Lou was there. Not because I was becoming a legend. Because I was his son. When I got pinned in nineteen seconds, he didn't see failure. He saw his kid. Nothing but unconditional love.

I have to say a word here about the two people woven through all of it. Charlie — the man whose one suggestion started everything — drifted into the disease a few years later, the same disease that would come for me. Lou was the one who went and got him, found him alone in his apartment the way you find someone who has stopped being able to take care of himself. Charlie lost that fight. He was the first person I loved who was just gone. What haunts me now is that Lou had already walked into a room like that — already knew it doesn't always end in a rescue — years before he ever drove to mine. He came for me anyway.

And Dave Meyer — my best friend from those years, the quarterback everybody liked, who went on to the NFL and then to China as a missionary and never once made me feel left out — is writing the foreword to this book. That tells you everything you need to know about who he is.

And then I left.
