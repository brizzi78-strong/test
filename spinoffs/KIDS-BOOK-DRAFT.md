# The Little Cardinal's Promise — picture book, first-draft text

**Format:** 32-page picture book, ~600 words, ages 4–8
**Position:** the book a hospice worker can hand a family with young kids.
Fiction, inspired by the cardinal; not the memoir's factual story.
**Needs:** illustrator (32 pages / ~15 spreads). Uses ISBN #5 from the block.
**Note for Rob:** names and details are placeholders — change anything.

---

SPREAD 1 (pages 4–5)
Mateo's grandpa was the kind of grandpa who showed up.
[Art: a boy and his grandpa in a driveway, ball gloves, an old red truck.]

SPREAD 2 (6–7)
He came to the games Mateo won, and the games Mateo lost.
He was always in the third row. Arms crossed. Watching.
[Art: bleachers, grandpa in the third row, boy waving from the field.]

SPREAD 3 (8–9)
Saturdays were for fly balls. A hundred of them, then a hundred more.
"One more," Grandpa always said. It was never just one more.
[Art: a park at golden hour, ball high in the sky.]

SPREAD 4 (10–11)
Grandpa made one promise, and only one.
"No matter what," he said, "I will always show up for you."
[Art: pinky promise, close-up, big hand and small hand.]

SPREAD 5 (12–13)
One winter, Grandpa got sick. His naps got longer. His voice got softer.
But when Mateo sat beside him, Grandpa still smiled the smile
that meant: you're mine, and I'm glad.
[Art: quiet bedroom, boy's drawings taped to the wall.]

SPREAD 6 (14–15)
Mama said Grandpa's body was tired, and that some tired doesn't get better.
"Then who will show up for me?" Mateo asked.
Grandpa heard him. He held out his hand.
"Watch for me," Grandpa whispered. "I keep my promises."
[Art: boy's hand in grandpa's, faces close.]

SPREAD 7 (16–17)
Grandpa died on a snowy day.
The house got quiet. The truck stayed parked.
Mateo didn't want breakfast. He didn't want fly balls.
He didn't want anything at all.
[Art: snow falling on the red truck, one lit window.]

SPREAD 8 (18–19)
The quiet stayed a long time.
[Art: wordless spread — winter, the empty third row of bleachers dusted
with snow. Let the picture carry it.]

SPREAD 9 (20–21)
One cold morning, Mateo heard a tap. Tap. Tap-tap.
[Art: boy looking up from cereal.]

SPREAD 10 (22–23)
On the window sat a bird. Not a brown bird. Not a gray bird.
A red bird. Red as Grandpa's truck.
It looked right at Mateo. And it stayed.
[Art: THE spread — brilliant cardinal on the snowy sill, boy's wide eyes.]

SPREAD 11 (24–25)
"Mama!" Mateo whispered. "It's not flying away!"
Mama knelt beside him, and her eyes went shiny.
"You know what some people say about red birds," she said.
"They say a cardinal is how love shows up... after."
[Art: mother and son at the window, bird holding their gaze.]

SPREAD 12 (26–27)
Mateo put his hand against the cold glass.
"You came," he said. "You kept your promise."
The cardinal tipped its head — the way Grandpa did
when he was proud and didn't say so.
[Art: small hand on glass, bird's tilted head. Mirror the pinky-promise pose.]

SPREAD 13 (28–29)
The cardinal didn't come every day. But it came.
On the first day of school. At the last game of the season.
On days when Mateo missed Grandpa so much his chest hurt —
tap. Tap-tap.
[Art: montage of seasons — backpack day, ballfield, rainy window.]

SPREAD 14 (30–31)
And when it came, Mateo always said the same thing.
"I see you. I love you too."
Because love that shows up doesn't stop showing up.
It just changes where it sits.
[Art: boy in the yard, cardinal in the branch above — the third row of the tree.]

SPREAD 15 (32)
Now, when the world feels too quiet... watch the windows.
Watch the fences and the branches and the snow.
Red keeps its promises.
[Art: final page — cardinal in flight over the neighborhood at dawn.]

THE END

Back matter (last page, small type): A note for grown-ups — one short paragraph
on talking with children about death honestly (say "died," not "went to sleep"),
letting kids see you miss the person, and letting signs be a comfort without
being a test. Point to the memoir for the whole story of the real cardinal.
