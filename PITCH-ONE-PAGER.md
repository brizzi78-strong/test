# The Cardinal's Promise — Pitch One-Pager

## Positioning sentence (say this first, always)
The Cardinal's Promise is a memoir about the father who kept showing up — for a son
he chose at eight, rescued at thirty, and shaped into the man who now sits with
strangers' families in hospice rooms — and about the red bird that landed on that
son's mirror fifteen days after the father died.

## Lead category
Father-son memoir (grief/love), with a recovery spine and a hospice vocation.
Pitch it as ONE book: the father-son story. Recovery and hospice are what make it
unlike the other father-son memoirs — they are the angle, not co-headliners.

## The angle (why it isn't "another addiction memoir")
There are many addiction memoirs and many grief memoirs. There are almost none in
which the love that survives the addiction becomes, verbatim, the author's
profession: the man rescued by a father with a U-Haul now walks into other
families' last rooms for a living. The book closes its own loop — Lou's method
(show up, stay, no bill) is now practiced on strangers, nightly.

## Comp titles (and the honest relationship to each)
- Beautiful Boy (David Sheff): the love-of-a-parent addiction story — ours is told
  from the son's side, and the parent stays.
- The Tender Bar (J.R. Moehringer): the men who raise a fatherless boy — ours has
  one man, and he arrives with Tootsie Rolls.
- Being Mortal (Gawande): the last-chapter conversation — ours from inside the
  rooms, by a man whose credential is having lived it.

## Reader takeaway (the sale-maker)
You cannot cure the people you love — not the addict, not the dying. You can stay.
Presence is cumulative; build it in the ordinary; say the great things now. The
back matter operationalizes this ("What I'm Asking You to Do" + Resources).

## Platform
Hospice liaison and Certified Dementia Practitioner; 17 years sober; speaks in
recovery rooms since year one (Gulf Breeze Hospital). Endorsements in hand:
foreword by Dave Meyer (Virginia Tech QB, NFL, coached under Saban at LSU);
back-cover quote from Dr. Huet; relationship with Quint Studer (named in the book).

## Landmines — never say these in a query or interview
1. NEVER describe the book as drawn from journals, diaries, or letters — fastest
   route to rejection. Research is invisible; the book is a narrative.
2. NEVER pitch it as "my father's story" or "a tribute to my dad." It is Rob's
   first-person transformation; Lou is the thread. (Educated and Beautiful Boy
   sold as the narrator's story, not the parent's biography.)
3. Never lead with the diagnosis list (adoption + addiction + grief + hospice).
   Lead with the one sentence at the top of this page.

## One-paragraph query pitch (ready to paste)
When Rob Brizzi was eight, he asked the man dating his mother if he could call him
Dad, and Lou Brizzi spent the next forty years answering that question with his
feet — driving to every match, every rehab, every wrecked apartment, a U-Haul to
West Palm Beach when his son was dying by inches. THE CARDINAL'S PROMISE (50,700
words) is the story of what that kind of love builds: a boy carried out of Mexico
City with nothing, a man who lost everything to addiction twice, and the hospice
worker he became — seventeen years sober, sitting with families in their last
rooms, practicing what his father taught him. Fifteen days after Lou died on
Christmas morning, a cardinal landed on Rob's driver's-side mirror and stayed.
This book is what it took to understand why.
