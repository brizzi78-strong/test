# Legal / Name-Risk Review — The Cardinal's Promise

Passages that name (or point at) real people in connection with crimes, drugs, or
organized-crime-adjacent stories. Status of each, and what a publication lawyer
should look at before press. The copyright page and Author's Note already disclose
that some names and identifying details have been changed — that disclosure is the
umbrella all pseudonyms live under.

## Handled in the manuscript

- **"Johnny Pascale" (The Suit, ch 8)** — surname removed; now "a boy at her
  school — I'll call him Johnny." A minor accused of bullying who gets knocked
  out was the sharpest risk on the list. If "Johnny Pascale" was already a
  pseudonym, no harm done; if it was real, it's gone.
- **The audit-job employer (The Suit, ch 8)** — already unnamed by design ("I
  won't use the name of the guy who hired me"). "Sal" and "Felicia" are first
  names only, forty-plus years back, with no employer or union local named.
- **The suits-for-a-favor source (The Suit, ch 8)** — already anonymous
  ("someone close to our family I won't name"), and the "man who carried weight
  in certain circles" is unnamed and undescribable. Low identifiability.

## Still open — decide before publication

1. **The Rascals growing weed (The Suit, ch 8).** The band is named, famous, and
   members are living. The scene is a seven-year-old's memory from ~1985.
   Recreational use decades ago is mild, but it is still a drug allegation
   attached to named living people. Options, strongest protection first:
   (a) cut the weed detail and keep the gold-records scene, which loses little;
   (b) blur it — "plants in the backyard I understood much later," letting the
   reader infer; (c) keep as-is and have a lawyer sign off (truth is a defense,
   but you'd be the one proving a forty-year-old memory).
2. **"Pretending to audit the mob" (The Suit, ch 8).** No person is identifiable,
   so defamation risk is low, but the line characterizes a real past employer's
   business as a front. A lawyer will likely pass it because no one can claim it
   points at them — confirm the employer's name appears nowhere in drafts, notes,
   or marketing.
3. **The birth-certificate / license story (My Brother's Keeper, ch 11).** Names
   only Rob and Chris. Both incidents are decades old and outside any statute
   concern worth worrying about, but Chris should read and OK the passage —
   family sign-off is cheaper than family friction.
4. **Union-book auditing.** Covered by item 2 — no union local, city office, or
   individual is identifiable. Keep it that way; do not add "color" details
   (addresses, local numbers) in future passes.

## General instructions for the legal read

- Truth, opinion, and non-identifiability are the three shields; every risky
  passage above should sit squarely under at least one.
- Anyone still living who is named near drugs, crime, or violence: Rascals
  members (item 1) are the only remaining case.
- Dick Vitale, Dave Meyer, Quint Studer, Dr. Huet appear only in positive
  contexts — no defamation exposure. RESOLVED (July 2026): Dr. Huet approved
  the cover quote as printed ("Rob's journey in sobriety has become a life of
  service.") and Dave Meyer signed off on the foreword as printed. Both
  confirmed by Rob.
- The Author's Note's name-change disclosure should stay in the front matter
  verbatim; it is doing legal work, not just courtesy.
