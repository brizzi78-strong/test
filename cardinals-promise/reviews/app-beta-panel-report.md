# App Beta Panel — What the Reviewers Want (July 2026)

Five simulated caregivers (same method as the book's reader panel: each
reviews *in character*, screen-referenced criticism required), pointed at
the **Cardinal's Toolkit companion app** — the five tabs (Today,
Checklists, Tools, Journal, Resources), on-device only, no account. The
question put to them was Rob's: *"Ask the reviewers what they want."*

## The panel

| Reviewer | Life situation | Screens they lived in | Would recommend |
|---|---|---|---|
| Denise, 54 | Mom post-stroke, now in a SNF; reviews from waiting rooms | Today, Checklists (First 24 Hours), Resources | 8 |
| Tom, 61 | Dad slowly declining; reluctant, low-patience phone user | Today, Tools, first-run | 7 |
| Marta, RN | 20 yrs home health/hospice; clinical lens | Checklists, Resources, Medication List | 8 |
| Grace, 68 | Widow, 14 mo post-hospice; tone & grief lens | Today reflection, Gentle Words, Journal | 9 |
| Kevin, 38 | Long-distance caregiver for Dad; plan-ahead engineer | Checklists (Documents), data safety, sharing | 7 |

**Recommend average: 7.8** — just under the book panel's 8.0 ship gate.
Every reviewer liked what's there; the gap is a small number of things
that aren't there yet. The tone, the checklists, and the new cardinal all
scored high. The misses cluster tightly — which is good news.

## What landed (keep, don't touch)

- **The new cardinal, 5–0.** "Finally looks like a friend, not a logo."
  (Tom) Grace: "He makes me want to open it."
- **Gentle Words + the Today reflection, 5/5 tone.** Grace: "The words
  sound like they were written by someone who's actually done this."
- **Checklist tier colors (red/purple/blue), 5/5 clarity.** Denise found
  "In the First 24 Hours" in one tap and cried a little — the good kind.
- **On-device privacy promise, praised by 4/5** — until they thought
  about what "on-device only" means for their data (see #2 below).
- **Breathe + Come Back to Now, 5/5** as-is. Marta: "correct, calm, no
  false medical claims — rare."

## What they want — two-reviewer patterns (the mandatory ones)

These are the asks **two or more reviewers raised independently**. Under
the panel's own rules, these are the real signal.

1. **A real Medication List, not just a checklist to tick.** (Denise +
   Marta) The "Medication List" today is a checklist of *reminders* about
   what a good list contains. What both a daughter and a nurse actually
   want is a place to **type the meds** — name, dose, when, why,
   prescriber — and hand the phone to the ER. Marta: "Bring the list, not
   the bottles' — the app tells them that, then doesn't give them the
   list." **Highest-value single request of the panel.**

2. **Tell me my data is safe — or let me get it out.** (Kevin + Grace,
   with Marta nodding) "On-device only" reassured them on privacy and then
   frightened them: *if I lose this phone, do I lose Mom's whole record and
   my journal?* Kevin: "No sync, no backup, no export = one dropped phone
   from zero." They want **either** iCloud backup **or** a plain
   **Export / Share** (PDF or text) of checklists, med list, and journal.
   Grace specifically wants to **print her journal**.

3. **Let me share the checklist with my sibling.** (Denise + Kevin) Care
   is rarely solo. A **Share** button on a checklist ("here's where we are
   on the documents folder") would matter more than any new feature. Even
   a read-only text export counts.

4. **"Parent" doesn't fit everyone.** (Grace + Tom) The About/care copy
   assumes an aging *parent*; Grace is caring for a spouse, and the book
   already fixed this ("wherever it says 'parent,' read 'the person you
   love'"). The app should carry the same line, and prefer **"the person
   you're caring for"** in UI copy.

5. **Bigger text, and don't make me hunt.** (Tom + Denise) Stressed,
   older, or teary eyes. Confirm the app respects **Dynamic Type** all the
   way up, and make the **crisis path** (988, "In the First 24 Hours")
   reachable from the very first screen without scrolling.

## What they want — high-value single-reviewer asks

6. **"Questions for the doctor" / appointment notes.** (Denise) A spot to
   jot what to ask before the visit and what was said after. "I forget
   half of it in the room."

7. **Add my own numbers to Resources.** (Marta) The national/NC list is
   good, but the number she needs at 2 a.m. is *this* parent's pharmacy,
   PCP, and on-call line. Let people **add their own contacts** to the
   Resources tab.

8. **Gentle, optional reminders.** (Kevin) Not nagging — one opt-in nudge:
   "check in?" or "refill due." He knows the app is deliberately quiet;
   he's asking for *opt-in*, off by default.

9. **A true first-run, 30 seconds.** (Tom) He didn't know what the app was
   for in the first 10 seconds. One warm intro card — "You are not alone.
   Start here." — pointing at the crisis checklist.

10. **Confirm-before-you-rely on resource numbers.** (Marta) Keep the
    "confirm the number" caution the book uses; numbers drift.

## If you only build three things before launch

The panel's rough consensus, in order:

1. **Export / backup** (pattern #2) — it's a *trust* fix, not a feature;
   without it the privacy promise reads as a data-loss risk.
2. **A fillable Medication List** (pattern #1) — the single most-wanted
   capability, and it's the app's own advice made real.
3. **Share a checklist** (pattern #3) — small effort, high daily value.

Everything else (own-contacts, appointment notes, optional reminders,
first-run card, spouse language) is a strong backlog — cheap wins to fold
in as time allows. #4 (inclusive "parent" language) and #5 (Dynamic Type
check) are near-free and could ship with any of the above.

## Method note

These are five *simulated* reviewers in the same tradition as the book's
beta panel — a structured way to pressure-test before real testers. They
are not a substitute for TestFlight. Every ask above should be re-checked
against real caregivers once a beta build is out; treat this as the
hypothesis, not the verdict.
