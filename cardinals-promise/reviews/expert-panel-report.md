# Expert Panel Report — *The Cardinal's Toolkit*

Five independent expert reviews of the full manuscript (172-page draft, July 2026):
a hospice/geriatric clinician, a Medicare/Medicaid benefits specialist, an
elder-law reviewer, a developmental editor reading as a literary agent, and a
family-caregiver reader.

## The headline

**Every reviewer's verdict was positive.** The agent-reader's answer to "would
you request the full manuscript?" was a qualified yes. The benefits specialist
found *no false statements of current law*. The clinician called it "an
unusually clinically sound caregiver handbook." The caregiver reader: "the book
I'd hand every family in the parking lot" — after the fixes below.

The work remaining is **targeted, not structural**: a handful of safety and
accuracy corrections, one weak chapter to rebuild, and three identity decisions
(title, North Carolina lane, AI disclosure).

---

## Consensus must-fixes (flagged by 3+ reviewers)

### 1. The AI-disclosure sentence (flagged by ALL FIVE)
Copyright page: *"All content should be reviewed, edited, and verified by the
author before publication"* reads as a leftover template instruction and
implies verification hasn't happened. Editorial's suggested replacement:

> "Portions of this book were drafted and organized with the assistance of AI
> tools. Every page has been reviewed, fact-checked, and revised by the authors
> — a Certified Dementia Practitioner and a Doctor of Pharmacy — and the
> experiences, judgments, and recommendations are entirely their own."

Also fix "the author's own" → "the authors' own" (copyright now credits both).

### 2. Rebuild Chapter 23 — Medical Orders (flagged by 4)
The DNR/MOST/POLST chapter is a third the length of its siblings, missing the
book's standard anatomy (no "What Families Usually Think," no "What to Do This
Week," no scripts), switches to first person, and its worksheet visually mimics
a real medical-order form **with no disclaimer** — legal's worst-case: a family
tapes the filled-in page to the refrigerator believing it's a DNR. Required:
- Full chapter anatomy and caregiver voice (or explicitly frame the worksheet
  as a page to hand the parent).
- Bold disclaimer: the worksheet is not a DNR/MOST/POLST and has no legal or
  medical effect; only a provider-signed order on the state's official form
  directs care.
- The operational facts: EMS follows portable medical orders, not living wills;
  the form must be visible/travel with the person; it can be changed or voided
  at any time; review at every care transition; intended for the seriously
  ill/frail, not every older adult.
- North Carolina uses the **MOST** form — say so; note POLST naming has moved
  to "Portable Orders…" and signature rules vary by state.

### 3. Resolve the North Carolina identity (flagged by 4)
The subtitle promises "The North Carolina Family Caregiver Handbook," but NC
appears only in the front matter — no Special Assistance, no CAP/DA, no SHIIP
contact, no NC MOST/POA execution specifics. Two lanes:
- **(a) Go national:** drop NC from the subtitle; add a "finding your state's
  version of these offices" page. (The trade-publishing answer.)
- **(b) Commit to NC as a series template:** add a 6–10 page NC resource
  appendix + NC callout boxes in Chs. 8, 14, 20, 21, 23 — then clone per state.
  (A legitimately smart self-pub play the architecture was built for.)
The current half-measure fails both.

### 4. Unify the title (editorial, echoed by others)
Three titles compete: cover (*The Cardinal's Toolkit: Caregiver Hero*), title
page (*Aging Parent Cardinal's Toolkit*), running identity (*The Cardinal's
Toolkit*). Recommendation: one title everywhere, keep "Cardinal" (series brand
+ memoir bridge), **drop "Caregiver Hero"** — it contradicts the book's own
de-heroizing ethos (Ch. 4's whole point).

## Safety must-fixes (clinician)

1. **Head injury + blood thinners is under-triaged (Ch. 2).** Currently in the
   "call the doctor" list. Must say: anticoagulated older adult who hits their
   head → emergency department the same day, even if they seem fine; watch
   24–72 hours for worsening headache, vomiting, confusion, unusual sleepiness.
2. **"Signs of stroke" is a 911 trigger used five times and never defined.**
   Add FAST/BE-FAST once (face drooping, arm weakness, speech trouble, sudden
   vision/balance loss — note the time, call 911).
3. **After-a-fall floor guidance (Ch. 2):** check before moving, keep warm,
   call EMS for a lift assist; a "long lie" (down an hour+) needs medical
   evaluation even without visible injury.

## Benefits must-fixes (specialist)

1. **Ch. 10 lists "progress" as a Medicare coverage condition** — repeats the
   *Jimmo v. Sebelius*-settled "improvement standard" myth. Drop "progress";
   add that skilled maintenance care qualifies and "plateaued" denials can be
   appealed.
2. **Ch. 12 must state the hospice election trade-off:** Medicare generally
   stops paying for curative treatment of the terminal illness while on hospice
   (other conditions continue; revocable). "None of that is true" overcorrects.
3. **Ch. 17 omits the Part D annual out-of-pocket cap** (post-2025) and the
   Medicare Prescription Payment Plan — the biggest drug-cost change in
   decades; add a hedged paragraph.
4. **Name the 3-day inpatient rule** in Ch. 13 (observation days and discharge
   day don't count; many MA plans waive it) and add observation-status +
   fast-appeal (QIO) cross-references in Ch. 10.
5. **Ch. 18:** add the VA pension 36-month look-back warning (mirror the
   Medicaid box); fix Form 21-2680 (physician's exam form) vs. 21P-527EZ (the
   application).
6. **Ch. 13:** "replace Original Medicare" → "receive your Medicare benefits
   through a private plan"; note Medigap underwriting risk when leaving MA.

## Reader-experience fixes (caregiver reader + editorial)

- **Move the triage to the front.** Quick Start and "What Are You Facing?" sit
  behind ~8 pages of preamble; merge them into one "Start Here" spread placed
  right after the color legend, and make the first 24 hours a *do* list, not a
  reading list. Merge the two "Before You Begin" essays (keep the Central Park
  story).
- **Make the colors and the routing agree** (RED routing page sends readers to
  YELLOW chapters) — or state plainly that colors mark topic urgency and the
  routing page is how you navigate in crisis.
- **Add a "Numbers That Matter" page** — Eldercare Locator, Alzheimer's 24/7
  Helpline (800-272-3900), 988, VA Caregiver Support Line, APS, NC SHIIP/DSS.
  "The most valuable page the book doesn't have."
- **De-duplicate:** one master contacts page instead of ~20 repeated worksheet
  headers; merge the near-identical Ch. 27/28 worksheets; keep the full home
  health vs. private duty treatment once (Ch. 6) and compress the other three.
- **Add back matter:** About the Authors, glossary, index, resource directory,
  proper memoir page (soften "early 2027" to "forthcoming").
- **Give Hope Brizzi a visible lane** — "Hope's Note" boxes (pharmacist's
  counterpart to Rob's Note) in Chs. 13, 17, 25, 27–28, or a stated "medically
  reviewed by" role. Strengthens both the book and the AI-disclosure answer.
- **Ch. 4 needs a crisis resource box** (988, "your own doctor," respite by
  name); wandering guidance should name the Alzheimer's Helpline and a locator
  program; memory-care tour list should add the antipsychotics question.
- Trim a few quips ("cage match in sensible shoes"), vary the "better question"
  formula in a third of chapters, and pair every "don't wait until the
  caregiver collapses" with the book's usual "this is not failure" counterweight.

## What the panel says to keep exactly as-is

- **The scripts** — "exact words at exactly the moments language fails."
  Unanimously the book's highest-value content.
- **The myth-first chapter engine** ("What Families Usually Think") — "never
  misfires."
- **Rob's Notes** — the emotional spine; Ch. 8's chandelier/2 a.m. call button,
  Ch. 9's "a fuse," Ch. 19's "mercy with tabs."
- **Chapter 4 (Caregiver Burnout)** — "the standout chapter and reason enough
  to buy the book," with the best worksheet (the scored self-check).
- **Chapters 27–29** — clinically correct, emotionally pitched exactly right;
  "do not let an editor soften or trim these." Ch. 29's "the right first call
  is rarely 911" prevents a second trauma.
- **The payer trapdoors** — "Medicare is health insurance, not long-term care
  insurance," skilled vs. custodial, the observation-status script — "better
  than most professional discharge-planning handouts."
- **The factual discipline** — hedged wording, almost no perishable dollar
  figures, questions-and-scripts over rules. "Would pass a SHIP/benefits-
  counselor review comfortably, which is rare for a consumer caregiving book."

3. Benefits corrections: Jimmo, hospice election, Part D cap, 3-day rule, VA.
4. Rebuild Chapter 23.
5. Decide title + NC lane (author decision), then execute it.
6. Front-matter consolidation, Numbers That Matter page, worksheet dedup.
7. Back matter, Hope's Notes, tone trims.
