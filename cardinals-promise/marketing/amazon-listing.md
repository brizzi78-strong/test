## Description (KDP-safe HTML)

```html
<b>Nobody picks the day they become a caregiver.</b> It arrives as a fall, a
diagnosis, or a discharge planner asking where your parent is going next — as
if you're supposed to already know. You're not. Almost no family is handed a map.

<b>This is the map.</b> Written by a Certified Dementia Practitioner with a
career across home health, hospice, and senior living, <i>The Caregiver's
Cardinal Toolkit</i> is built to be useful on your worst day — not read cover
to cover. Its 29 chapters are color-coded by urgency — <b>RED: Act Today ·
YELLOW: Watch Closely · BLUE: Plan Ahead</b> — so you can find the right page
in under a minute.

Inside, you'll learn how to:
<ul>
<li>Get medical, legal, and financial essentials into one Important Documents Folder — before a crisis scatters them</li>
<li>Ask the exact questions that protect Medicare coverage, including the observation-status trap that quietly voids rehab benefits</li>
<li>Compare every care option — home health, assisted living, memory care, skilled nursing, palliative care, hospice — and how families actually pay for each</li>
<li>Use word-for-word scripts for the hardest conversations with doctors, discharge planners, and siblings</li>
<li>Recognize caregiver burnout before it breaks you — because a plan that protects your parent but breaks you is not a real plan</li>
</ul>

Every chapter follows the same anatomy: the plain truth, the myth families walk
in believing, the teaching, and the tools — fillable worksheets, scored
self-checks, and a "What to Do This Week" list you can actually finish.

You don't have to be a superhero. The hero in this guide is the son or daughter
who shows up, asks sharper questions, and knows when to call in help. This book
makes sure you know what to ask.

<b>Large 8.5" × 11" full-color workbook format</b> — write in it, copy the
worksheets, take them to appointments. Medication content reviewed by Hope
Brizzi, PharmD. Book one of the Cardinal's Promise series.
```

## Copy rule for future marketing (website, A+ content, social)

Never pluralize the promise into "you're a hero" affirmation marketing.
The construction is always: **the hero is the one who shows up and asks for
help** — that's what reconciles the subtitle with Chapter 4's message.
