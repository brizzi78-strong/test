# The Hours Between

*An original piece written in the style of David Sheff's memoir "Beautiful Boy." All characters and events are invented. Nothing here is excerpted from or based on the Sheff family's story.*

---

The phone is face-up on the kitchen table and I am watching it the way you watch water that will not boil. It is 2 a.m. Eli said he would call at ten.

There was a time when I knew where my son was every hour of every day, because he was small enough to carry, and where I was, he was. I knew the weight of him asleep on my chest. I knew the smell of his scalp, like warm bread. When he was six he asked me whether the moon followed our car specifically or every car, and I told him every car, and he was quiet for a while in the backseat and then said, *that's a lot of work for the moon.* I have been living on that sentence for four months now. That sentence, and the hours between phone calls.

Here is what I have learned, because when your child is using, you learn things. You become a reluctant scholar of a subject you never wanted to study. Fentanyl is fifty times more potent than heroin. Two milligrams — a few grains of salt — can be lethal. The counselors talk about the adolescent brain, the prefrontal cortex that will not finish wiring itself until Eli is twenty-five, and I write it all down in a spiral notebook as if the notes will be on the test, as if there is a test, as if passing it would bring him home.

I used to believe that information was protection. I am a person who reads the studies, who calls the researchers, who asks the follow-up question. I thought if I understood the mechanism I could interrupt it. But addiction does not care what the father knows. It is not impressed by the notebook.

Rachel says I have to stop answering the phone with panic in my voice. "He can hear it," she says. "He calls less because of it." She is probably right. She is usually right, and I resent her for it in the small, ashamed way that people who are drowning resent the ones who can still swim.

The first time we found the pills, Eli was sixteen, and I sat on the edge of his bed and gave a speech I had been rehearsing since before he was born, the speech every parent keeps in a drawer, and he looked at me with such convincing remorse — he cried, I cried, we ordered Thai food — that I believed the whole thing was over. A parent's capacity for believing it is over is the renewable resource that addiction runs on.

Relapse is part of recovery. Everyone says this. The counselors say it, the books say it, the other parents in the church basement on Tuesday nights say it, holding their coffee in both hands. I understand it as a fact the way I understand the distances between stars. It does not survive contact with the specific night, the specific silence, the specific phone on the specific table.

What did I do wrong? The counselors tell me not to ask this. The three C's, they say: you didn't cause it, you can't control it, you can't cure it. I recite this. I also lie awake performing the archaeology of fatherhood — the divorce when he was nine, the year I traveled too much for work, the beer I let him sip at a wedding because it seemed harmless, because everything seems harmless until the moment it reveals itself to have been a door.

And yet. This is the thing I cannot explain to anyone who has not stood in it: alongside the fear, intact, untouched, is the boy. The fear does not evict him. Eli at eight, doing his homework at this same table, tongue between his teeth. Eli at twelve, teaching himself guitar from videos, the same four chords through the ceiling every night until I hated the song and loved it. Eli last spring — clean, then, or trying — standing in the driveway telling me about the ocean, how he wanted to see it again, and for a moment he was so entirely himself, so *present*, that I let myself believe the boy and the addict were separate people and that the boy was winning.

They are not separate people. That is the cruelest part of the design.

At 2:40 the phone lights up. I let it ring twice so he cannot hear how fast I moved.

"Dad," he says. His voice is tired but it is his voice, the true one, the one I can read like weather. "Sorry it's late. I'm okay. I'm at the house. I made curfew."

"That's good," I say, as evenly as I can manage, Rachel's coaching holding up under live conditions. "That's really good, El."

We talk about nothing for four minutes — the food there, a guy in his group from Fresno, whether I paid the phone bill, which I did, which I will always do, because the phone bill is the thread. Then he says he has to go, and he says, "Love you," in the fast mumbled way of boys, and the line clicks, and I sit in the kitchen with the silence, which has changed its composition entirely, and is now something a person could almost breathe.

He is okay tonight. I have given up on the word *forever*; I have negotiated all the way down to *tonight*, and I have discovered that tonight, held correctly, is enough to live on. The moon follows every car. It's a lot of work for the moon. It does it anyway.
