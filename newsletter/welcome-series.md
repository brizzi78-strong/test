## Email 1 — Day 0
**Subject:** Your guide is here — and one thing to know first
**Preview:** You reached out. That already matters.

Your free guide is ready — it's waiting for you back on the site, and you can print it or keep it on your phone.

But before you read it, I want to tell you one thing.

Nobody teaches you how to do this. Caregiving, grief, helping a child through loss — you're showing up untrained, mid-crisis, like all of us do. That isn't a failure. That's the situation. And you're already doing the most important part: you're here.

My father, Lou, died on Christmas Day, 2025. I'd spent years sitting with families in hospice — I knew every protocol, I had the credentials. And when it was my own dad, I still didn't have the right words half the time.

What I had was presence. I stayed in the room.

That's the whole point of everything I write. You don't have to be perfect at this. You have to be there.

Over the next couple of weeks I'll send you a few short notes — the things I wish someone had told me. No pressure, and you can reply to any of them. I read every one.

## Email 2 — Day +2
**Subject:** The mistake I see caregivers make most
**Preview:** It's the one that feels like strength.

The most common mistake I see isn't medical. It's this: trying to carry the whole thing alone.

It feels like strength. It feels like love. "I've got it. Don't worry about me." And it quietly takes people apart.

You are not built to be the doctor, the nurse, the scheduler, the cook, the driver, and the only one who ever cries — all at once, indefinitely.

So here's your one job today: name a single task and hand it to someone else. Let a friend bring a meal. Let a sibling make the pharmacy calls. Say yes to the person who asked "what can I do?" with an actual answer.

Accepting help isn't giving up. It's how you stay in the room for the long haul.

## Email 3 — Day +5
**Subject:** When the words won't come
**Preview:** You don't need the perfect thing to say.

People freeze at the bedside, or in front of someone grieving, because they're waiting to think of the perfect thing to say.

There isn't one. And you don't need it.

"I'm here." "I love you." "You don't have to talk." "Tell me about him." Silence, with your hand on theirs.

That's enough. More than enough. The people who feel most alone aren't the ones who heard the wrong words — they're the ones everyone avoided because no one knew what to say.

Don't avoid. Show up clumsy. Presence beats eloquence every single time.

If it helps, the guide you downloaded has a few of these lines ready to borrow. Keep it close.

## Email 4 — Day +9
**Subject:** For the one no one is checking on
**Preview:** Who's taking care of you?

A quick, honest question: who is checking on you?

Caregivers and grieving people become invisible fast. Everyone asks about the patient, the person who died, the kids. Almost no one asks about you.

So I will. How are you — really?

Three small things, today:
1. Eat something and drink water. You can't pour from an empty cup.
2. Pick one person to be your "I'm not okay" call, and tell them they're the one.
3. Save this number, before you need it: in the US, call or text **988**, any time, day or night.

You matter in this story too. Not just for what you do for someone else — you, on your own.

You might have wondered about the bird.

A cardinal is a long-standing sign of remembrance — the idea that someone you loved is near, that they haven't fully left. When my dad died, I started noticing them. I won't tell you what to believe about that. I'll just say it helped.

That's the spirit behind everything here: *The Cardinal's Promise* (a story to sit with you in the hardest season) and *The Cardinal's Toolkit* (the practical companion — scripts, checklists, and rituals for the hard days). The free guide you grabbed is one small piece of it.

If any of these notes landed, I'd love to hear from you — just reply and tell me where you are in this. I read every message.
