CHAPTER 10: West Palm Beach

The disease doesn't kick the door in. It moves in slowly, pays the rent for a while, and then one day you realize it owns the place and you're the one sleeping on the floor.

I moved to West Palm Beach because Kim's condo had run its course and I needed somewhere to go. That's the honest version. There was no plan. There was no job worth the name. There was distance — from Nancey, from the couch and the banana cream pies and the Dateline marathons that had kept me tethered to something. Boca had been a holding pattern. West Palm was where I stopped holding.

The apartment was a one-bedroom on the ground floor. I want to describe it accurately because for a long time I described everything about that period in a way that made it sound more manageable than it was. The blinds stayed drawn. Not some days. Every day. Light felt like an accusation. There was a refrigerator, but at some point it stopped working — or I stopped paying the electric, I genuinely can't tell you which came first — and I pushed it out into the hallway because the smell of a dead refrigerator in a small apartment is its own kind of company you don't want. So that was the picture, if anyone had looked: a grown man, thirty years old, blinds drawn at two in the afternoon, a refrigerator standing in the hallway like a tombstone, and inside, nothing.

I wasn't eating much. The pills took the appetite and the alcohol took whatever the pills missed. I'd been a blackout drinker from the very first time I drank — not giggly after two, just gone, lights out, piece-the-night-together-from-other-people's-faces-in-the-morning. By West Palm there were no other people's faces. There was just me, which meant the nights stopped getting pieced together at all. They just disappeared. I'd come up out of one and not know what day it was, and the not-knowing stopped scaring me, which is the part that should have scared me most.

Here is the thing nobody tells you about the bottom: it is unbelievably boring. The movies make it look dramatic — the squalor, the chaos, the rock-bottom scene with the swelling music. The truth is that it is small and gray and repetitive. You wake up sick. You fix the sickness. You wait for the fix to turn on you, which it always does, faster every time, because the math runs in one direction and never reverses. The edge you bought on Monday is Tuesday's normal and Wednesday you're already reaching for it again. You are not chasing a high anymore. You stopped chasing the high a long time ago. You are just trying to stop the floor from coming up to meet you, and the only tool you have for that is the same thing that is pulling the floor up in the first place.

I had been someone. That's the part my mind kept circling, in the few lucid stretches between. Cintas. C.R. Bard. Six figures by twenty-four. The Abercrombie commercial that ran in every store in America, my face on a loop in a thousand malls. County champion at seventeen, the name on the wall. And now I was a man who could not keep a refrigerator running. The distance between those two people was so wide that the only way I knew how to handle it was to not look at it, and the most reliable way to not look at it was already in my hand.

I lied to my mother on the phone. I want to own that plainly. She'd call and I'd assemble the old voice — the confident one, the one that closes deals — and tell her I was fine, things were turning around, I had some leads. She'd been the woman who carried two boys across a border on nothing but courage, and I was lying to her from a dark apartment because I couldn't stand to add my weight to what she was already carrying. Chris knew more than I gave him credit for. He'd sat me down at one point and given me the ultimatum — admit you have a problem or admit you have a mental health issue, pick one — and I wasn't even angry, because I respected him too much for that. Even through the fog I knew my brother was trying to save me. I just wasn't reachable yet.

And Lou. Lou I lied to least, because Lou was the hardest to lie to. He didn't ask a lot of questions. He'd never been a man who interrogated. He'd just call, and listen, and I could feel him on the other end of the line doing the thing he'd done in the wrestling stands for four years — reading me, deciding whether I could recover from the position I was in. The conversations got shorter. I'd keep them short on purpose, because the longer they ran, the more likely the truth was to slip out, and I wasn't ready to hand him the truth. Handing Lou the truth meant it became real. It meant the foundation he'd spent thirty years building — the pool, the batting cages, the third row, the last twenty dollars he never told me about until years later — was being torn down by my own hands while he watched.

What I didn't understand then, and understand completely now, is that he already knew. He'd done this before. He had driven to an apartment once, years earlier, and found Charlie alone in the dark the way you find someone who has stopped being able to take care of himself. He knew the smell of it. He knew the geography of a life that has emptied out. And he knew — because Charlie didn't make it — that love does not always win, that you can drive to the apartment and knock on the door and do everything right and still lose. He knew all of that, and he was on the phone with me anyway, getting quieter, doing the math a father does when he can hear his son disappearing.

There was a night near the end of it — I couldn't tell you the date, the dates were gone by then — when I sat on the floor of that apartment with the refrigerator in the hallway and the blinds drawn against an afternoon I'd already let turn into night, and I understood, in a flat and unremarkable way, that I was going to die there. Not as a fear. As a fact, the way you note the weather. I wasn't going to do anything dramatic about it. I was just going to keep doing exactly what I was doing until it finished the job, and the job was close.

I don't know what I said on the phone the last time before he came. I've tried to remember and I can't. Maybe I finally let something slip. Maybe he just heard, in the spaces between the words, the thing I'd been working so hard to keep out of them. Maybe he didn't need me to say anything at all. He'd been reading me from the third row since I was twelve.

What I know is that I hung up, and somewhere a thousand miles north a man in his fifties — a man who had married my mother and then watched that marriage end, a man who owed Claudio Balderas exactly nothing — made a decision. He didn't call to announce it. He didn't ask if it was okay. That wasn't how Lou worked.

He just came.
