# The Promise

## *A Name to Grow Into*

**Rob Brizzi** · Brizzi House Publishing · 2026

A 10-page picture book told in short, plain, declarative lines — the voice of a
grown man speaking back to the father who chose him. Pages marked **[existing
art]** use the illustrations in `art/`; their text is kept word-for-word from
the finished pages. The remaining pages are written to match.

### Page 1 — THE DAY YOU PICKED ME

Some kids come to their fathers
the ordinary way.

I didn't.

You looked at me,
and you picked me.

On purpose.

> *Illustration: The man crouched to a small boy's eye level, hand out,
> the boy deciding. Front hallway light.*

---

### Page 2 — THE NAME

You gave me your name.

It was too big for me then,
like your coat.

"Don't worry," you said.
"It's a name to grow into."

> *Illustration: The boy swallowed up in the father's coat, sleeves past his
> hands, both of them grinning at the mirror.*

---

### Page 3 — [existing art: `art/father-and-boy.jpeg`]

You didn't teach me with speeches.

You taught me by being there.

Again.
And again.
And again.

> *The boy in the red shirt on his father's lap, looking up at him,
> mid-story, mid-laugh.*

---

### Page 4 — WHAT STAYING LOOKS LIKE

Sitting in the bleachers.
Sitting in the waiting room.
Sitting on the edge of my bed
on the nights I couldn't say why I was sad.

You never fixed it from the doorway.

You always came in
and sat down.

> *Illustration: Three small panels — bleachers, waiting room, edge of the
> bed — the father in the same posture in each: sitting, close, unhurried.*

---

### Page 5 — THE HARD YEARS

I got lost for a while.
Most people would have stopped looking.

You didn't.

You came and got me.
Like the first time.

On purpose.

> *Illustration: A long road at dusk. Headlights. The father's silhouette
> stepping out of the truck. Kept simple and unspecific — the child reader
> sees "lost"; the grown reader will know.*

---

### Page 6 — GROWN

One day the coat fit.

One day the name fit, too.

And I understood
that you hadn't just given me a name.

You'd shown me what to do with it.

> *Illustration: The narrator, grown now, in the blue shirt, wearing the old
> coat. It fits. The father, gray at the temples, watching him.*

---

### Page 7 — THE WORK I DO NOW — [existing art: `art/the-work-i-do-now.jpeg`]

Years later,
I sit beside people
who are saying
goodbye.

Families.
Grandfathers.
Mothers.

People who
are scared.

I stay.

Because you
taught me how.

> *The narrator, hospice badge on a lanyard, holding an elderly patient's
> hands at his bedside. Warm lamplight.*

---

### Page 8 — THE PROMISE — [existing art: `art/the-promise.jpeg`]

You picked me once.

Now I spend my life
picking others.

Sitting down.

Showing up.

Staying.

The promise
is still being kept.

> *The two men side by side now — the father in the green plaid, older,
> the son grown in the blue shirt, the father's arm around his shoulders.*

---

### Page 9 — A NAME TO GROW INTO

Someday, somebody small
might get my name.

It'll be too big for them at first.

That's all right.

I know exactly
what to tell them.

> *Illustration: The narrator's hand resting on an empty chair beside a
> bed — or, gentler, holding the old coat out toward the reader. Open,
> hopeful, unfinished on purpose.*

---

### Dedication (back page)

*For the man who picked me —
and for everyone still sitting down,
showing up,
and staying.*

## Production notes

- **Existing art:** three finished illustrations live in `art/`
  (`father-and-boy.jpeg`, `the-work-i-do-now.jpeg`, `the-promise.jpeg`).
  The cover composition (title panel over the father-and-boy image) already
  exists in the original generation session and can be re-exported at print
  resolution.
- **Art still needed:** pages 1, 2, 4, 5, 6, and 9 — six new images in the
  same style (soft watercolor-and-ink, warm cream backgrounds, the same two
  characters aging across the book; the red shirt and the coat are the
  recurring visual anchors).
- **Page 5** is the pivot for adult readers. The text stays child-safe —
  "lost" is all a young reader needs — while carrying the full weight of the
  rescue for anyone who lived it.
- **Voice rule for any edits:** short declarative lines, one clause each,
  periods as beats. No rhyme. The word "stay" (and its forms) is the spine of
  the book — it should appear on pages 4, 7, and 8, and nowhere else, so it
  lands each time.
