# The Reddest Bird

*A picture book about love, loss, and the little red visitor who reminds us*

**Audience:** ages 4–8 (picture book, ~32 pages / 14 spreads)
**Word count:** ~550 words
**Themes:** grief, remembering, the cardinal tradition — "when a cardinal appears, a loved one is near"

---

## Manuscript

*Text is laid out spread by spread. Illustration notes are suggestions, not instructions — a good illustrator will find better ones.*

---

### Spread 1

Grandma Rose knew everything about birds.

She knew which ones whistled and which ones warbled,
which ones hopped and which ones strutted,
and which ones stole the dog's kibble when nobody was looking.

> *Illustration: A backyard bursting with birds. Grandma Rose and June, a small girl with untied shoelaces, filling a feeder together. A blue jay making off with a piece of kibble.*

---

### Spread 2

But her favorite — her very favorite —
was the cardinal.

"Look, June," she'd whisper. "The reddest bird.
Red as a mitten. Red as a wagon.
Red as the jam we never wipe off our chins."

> *Illustration: Close-up of Grandma Rose and June at the window, cheeks nearly touching. Outside, a brilliant male cardinal on a snowy branch.*

---

### Spread 3

Every morning, all winter long,
the cardinal came to the feeder,
and Grandma Rose and June watched him
with jam on their chins.

> *Illustration: A string of little vignettes — the two of them at the window in pajamas, in party hats, wrapped in one giant blanket. The cardinal in every scene.*

---

### Spread 4

Then one morning, the house was quiet.

Too many coats in the hallway.
Too many casseroles on the counter.
Too many grown-ups speaking in soft, slow voices.

Grandma Rose was gone.

> *Illustration: June small on the stairs, chin on her knees, watching a hallway of grown-ups' legs and coats. Muted colors. No red anywhere on this spread.*

---

### Spread 5

June didn't want breakfast.
She didn't want her red mittens.
She didn't even want jam.

"Gone *where*?" she asked.

Nobody had a good answer.

> *Illustration: June at the kitchen table, toast untouched. Her mother kneeling beside her chair, holding her hand, eyes tired and kind.*

---

### Spread 6

The feeder went empty.
The window went unwatched.
The winter went on and on and on.

> *Illustration: The empty feeder against a white-gray sky. The window from outside, curtains half closed. Small June-shaped silhouette behind the glass.*

---

### Spread 7

Until one morning —

*tap. tap. tap.*

> *Illustration: Nearly all white space. Just the corner of the window, and the suggestion of something small and red at its edge. Page-turn suspense.*

---

### Spread 8

There, on the feeder,
sat the reddest bird June had ever seen.

Red as a mitten.
Red as a wagon.
Red as the jam we never wipe off our chins.

> *Illustration: FULL-BLEED. The cardinal, magnificent, filling the spread with the first real red since Spread 3. June's astonished face reflected faintly in the glass.*

---

### Spread 9

"Mama!" June shouted. "He came back!
But — who will tell him things?
Who will know if he whistles or warbles?
Who will watch him with me?"

> *Illustration: June pulling her mother by the hand toward the window, pointing, talking so fast her words tumble.*

---

### Spread 10

Her mother looked for a long, quiet moment.

"You will," she said.
"Grandma Rose put everything she knew about birds
into you.
That's what loving someone does.
It doesn't go away.
It goes *into* you."

> *Illustration: Mother and daughter at the window, the mother's hand on June's chest, right over her heart. The cardinal watching them both.*

---

### Spread 11

So June filled the feeder.

She learned which birds whistled and which ones warbled,
which ones hopped and which ones strutted,
and exactly which one
still stole the dog's kibble.

> *Illustration: June in Grandma Rose's too-big gardening hat, scooping seed, surrounded by birds. The blue jay, mid-heist. Color returning to the world — early spring.*

---

### Spread 12

And every time the cardinal came,
June whispered what Grandma Rose used to whisper —

"Hello, reddest bird."

And it always, always felt
like somebody whispered back.

> *Illustration: June at the window in golden morning light, one hand on the glass. The cardinal close on the other side, head tilted, as if listening.*

---

### Spread 13

Some people say that when a cardinal appears,
someone you love is near.

June doesn't know if that's true.

> *Illustration: Wide, peaceful spread of the backyard in full spring. June on the porch steps with toast and jam, the cardinal on the feeder.*

---

### Spread 14

But every morning, all year long,
the reddest bird comes to her feeder.

And every morning, June has jam on her chin.

*The end.*

> *Illustration: Final image — June grinning, jam on her chin, and beside her on the railing, close as a secret, the cardinal. On the back endpapers: a child's field-guide page of "Birds I Know," in June's handwriting, signed "June & Grandma Rose."*

## A Note for Grown-Ups

Children grieve in bursts — sad one minute, playing the next. Both are grief,
and both are okay.

This story doesn't promise children that a bird *is* their person. It offers
something sturdier: the people we love leave what they knew and what they
loved inside us, and remembering together — out loud, often, with jam on our
chins — is how love keeps going.

Ways to use this book:

- **Let the child ask "gone where?"** You don't need a perfect answer. "I miss
  her too, and I'm here" is enough.
- **Find your family's cardinal.** A song, a recipe, a walk, a bird at the
  feeder — any small ritual that says *we still remember.*
- **Say the name.** Children take their cue from us. Saying "Grandma Rose
  loved this" teaches them that remembering is safe, not sad-forbidden.

If a child's grief keeps them from eating, sleeping, or playing for a long
stretch, a pediatrician or a children's grief center (such as The Dougy
Center, dougy.org) can help.
