CHAPTER 11: The U-Haul

There was a knock on the door.

I want to stay in that moment for a second, because I have stayed in it for seventeen years and I expect I will stay in it for the rest of my life. The blinds were drawn. I didn't know what time it was. I wasn't expecting anyone — I'd stopped expecting anyone. And there was a knock, steady and unhurried, the knock of a man who has driven a long way and is not leaving.

I opened the door and Lou was standing there.

He'd flown into Florida and rented a U-Haul. He had not called ahead to confirm I needed him, the same way he hadn't called ahead the time he got in his car in New Jersey and drove ten hours to Ohio because he heard something wrong in my voice on a college phone call. He just came. He stood in the doorway of that apartment and he looked at me — at the weight I'd lost, at the color I didn't have, at whatever a father sees when he finally lays eyes on the thing he's been hearing in the silences on the phone — and he didn't flinch. He didn't perform shock. He didn't say the thing you'd brace for. He looked past me into the dark apartment, took in the drawn blinds and the refrigerator out in the hallway, and the only thing on his face was the thing that had always been on his face. He'd found me. That was the assignment. He'd completed it.

I don't remember everything he said. I remember he came in. I remember he didn't lecture. There was a man who had every right to stand in that doorway and itemize the cost of me — the money, the worry, the broken trust, the years — and he did not say one word of it. He had carried all of that quietly his whole life and he wasn't going to set it down on me now, not in that room, not in the shape I was in. Lou never made me feel like I was too expensive to love. Even there. Even then. Especially then.

He took the couch. I keep coming back to that detail because it's the whole man in one decision. The apartment had one bed and one ruined couch, and the bed was mine, and a man in his fifties who'd driven a U-Haul a thousand miles to rescue a son who wasn't his by blood lay down on the broken couch so I could have the bed. He didn't announce it as a sacrifice. He was constitutionally uninterested in announcing sacrifice. He just took the couch the way he'd taken everything — without comment, as if it were the normal texture of being a father.

In the morning he loaded what was left of my life into the back of that truck. There wasn't much. That's a strange thing to discover about yourself — how little is actually left when the disease has been selling off the furniture for years. We loaded it anyway, every box and bag of it, and I remember the daylight when we finally pulled the blinds and opened the door for real, how it came into that apartment like it had been waiting outside the whole time for permission. The refrigerator stayed in the hallway. We left it where it stood, a tombstone for the man I'd very nearly become in those rooms.

And then we drove north.

Eleven hours, give or take, the back of that cargo van loaded with my whole diminished life, heading up through Florida in the dark. I don't romanticize that drive. I was sick. I was scared in the specific way you're scared when the thing that has been running your life is suddenly being driven away from at sixty-five miles an hour and you don't know who you are without it. But I was in the passenger seat of a truck my father was driving, and there is a particular safety in that — the safety I'd felt as a four-year-old on an airplane out of Mexico City with my mother's hand on mine, the complete, uncomplicated trust that wherever we were going, someone who loved me had thought it through.

Here is the part I find almost unbearable now, the part I didn't have the room to feel at the time. Lou had done this before. He had driven to an apartment and walked into the dark and tried to bring someone home, and it hadn't been enough. Charlie didn't make it. Lou knew, getting into that U-Haul, that love does not always win. He knew you can do everything right and still lose the person. He had the data. He'd buried the result. And he came anyway — for the second boy from Mexico City, the one whose mother had long since moved on, the one society would have told him he was free of. He got in the truck knowing exactly how this can end, and he drove eleven hours into the dark to find out which way it would go this time.

We stopped on the way. Not on that first long haul — that was the rescue. But in the days after, when the decision had been made and there was no more pretending, he drove me to rehab. And on the way, my father pulled into a Carrabba's.

He bought me a meal, and he let me have a last glass of wine.

I have turned that over more times than almost anything else in this whole story. A man driving his son to get sober, who stops at an Italian chain restaurant and lets the kid have one more, because he understood — without the language of treatment, without a single counselor's word for it — that you don't shove a starving, terrified person through a door, that there is a humanity in a last ordinary meal, that mercy and boundary can sit at the same table. He wasn't enabling me. We both knew where the car was pointed when we got back in it. He was just refusing, even at the threshold of the hardest thing, to treat me as anything less than his son having dinner with his father. One glass of wine at a Carrabba's. The last one. He let me have it, and then he drove me the rest of the way.

People hear that I got sober and they imagine a moment of clarity, a white light, a decision I made in the dark all by myself. That's not what happened. What happened is that a man I had spent years disappointing got on a plane, rented a truck, slept on a broken couch, loaded my life into a van, drove me a thousand miles, bought me one last meal, and delivered me to the door of the place where I would finally learn to save myself — because he had already learned, in the hardest possible way, that he couldn't do that part for me.

He could only get me to the door.

What did Lou Brizzi owe Claudio Balderas at that point? Nothing. He owed me nothing. He got on the plane anyway. He rented the U-Haul anyway. He drove me home.

Love is a verb. I had heard people say that. In the back of that cargo van, heading north through Florida in the dark, I was being driven somewhere by the definition of it.
