# Chapter 1: Mexico City, 1982 — The Escape

At four years old in Mexico City, my world was small and loud and warm, and I didn't know yet that it was about to end.

I remember shoes hanging from clotheslines strung between buildings. Vendors selling food on the street, music playing. The apartment was small even for my size. A short doorway, walls with a stucco feel. It smelled of tortillas and fresh laundry. My mother kept her own music playing from the kitchen while she cooked, and when the music played, I danced. I just moved. The music came, and I answered it.

My cousin Marcelo had a birthday party. The first piñata I ever saw. When it broke open and all the candy came out, I was fascinated.

I was a happy child. My mother and my father Horacio were the reason. She would put Chris on her hip and sing The Locomotion in her apron, dancing us around the kitchen.

She was thirty-one years old. Blond, striking, and visibly different in a country of dark hair and darker features. People said she looked like Sharon Stone, and they weren't wrong. She didn't shrink, even when everything around her was designed to make a woman in her position feel small.

Five years earlier, at twenty-six, she had done something that still strikes me as almost reckless in the best possible way. She moved to Mexico without knowing the language or the culture, following a man she loved into a life she didn't yet understand. She taught herself fluent Spanish through full immersion and stubbornness. As she told me later, she learned a lot of it from soap operas.

Horacio was my biological father. He had big highs and deep lows. One day he came home so excited he picked me up off the floor. He had gotten a promotion. Claudio, you are my good luck charm, he said. My mother made carne asada tacos and Mexican wedding cake. Another day he came home quiet, excused himself to the bedroom, and slept for what felt like three days. You never knew which version of him would walk through the door. My mother did everything she could. She didn't leave easily, and she didn't leave quickly. At some point, Horacio stood on the edge of his own despair and walked away from it in the only direction he could see.

When my mother was pregnant, I asked her to carry me. She said, I can't. I'm having a baby. And it's your baby. When she came home from the hospital carrying Chris, I looked at her and asked, "Is that my baby?" She smiled and said, "Yes, he wants you to hold him." I made an attempt, but he started crying, so I said, Mom, I'll try again later.

Chris cried a lot as a baby. Ear infections, one after another. My mother held him for hours, walking him, trying to figure out what he needed. I wanted her attention those nights. So I lay in my room and played my record player. Horacio had bought it for me. I played the same song over and over.

My mother made a decision. She told me we were going to visit my grandfather in America.

I had no framework for what that meant. The last time I saw Horacio was in the hallway. He had the newspaper in his hand. Have fun, he said. I'll see you in two weeks. My mother acted normal. She kissed him and said I love you. I hugged him and kissed him. I was wearing the Mickey Mouse t-shirt he had bought me, slightly too small. I didn't know it was goodbye. Not the permanent kind.

I remember the airport as noise and motion and my mother's hand, always my mother's hand. She had her suitcase and carried Chris. I carried my little red suitcase. The airplane I remember more clearly. The seatbelt that felt enormous around my small body. The sound of the engines building into something louder than anything I had encountered in a life of loud things. The way the whole vessel shuddered and then lifted, and then the city was below us, shrinking. My mom taught me to pop my ears and I thought I was going to rupture them. I blew so hard.

I don't remember fear. I remember trust. Complete, uncomplicated, four-year-old trust that wherever we were going, she had thought it through.

We landed in New Jersey in early winter and everything changed at once.

The language shifted first. Spanish disappeared almost overnight. Not gradually, not in stages. One day it was the only language I had, and then it wasn't. English surrounded me, sharp and fast and assembled according to rules I didn't yet have. I understood nothing, then pieces, and then, slowly, the pieces became a language I lived in without ever quite losing the ghost of the one I'd been born into. I still had a lisp that would send me to speech therapy twice a week ("th," "zzzz," over and over). With the language went pieces of myself I didn't know how to name yet.

The air felt colder. I could see my breath, which was a first. The trees had orange leaves and snow covered the ground. As far as I knew, Newark Airport was what America looked like.

At the airport, my Aunt Nancey picked us up in her little red Honda Civic. She took us to my grandparents' home in West Paterson, a quiet house, no chaos, no shouting, just routines and quiet evenings that felt enormous after the constant motion of Mexico City. It was just my mother, Chris, and me. I missed Horacio.

My mother began the slow work of rebuilding a life from nothing, without a formal education, without guarantees, carrying two small boys and the full weight of their future. She just worked. This book is not her story. She deserves her own, told in her own words, and I hope she writes it someday. But this book would not exist without her willingness to choose us, again and again, when doing so required courage she never asked to be recognized for.

She gave me the first thing a person needs before they can become anything. A chance.
