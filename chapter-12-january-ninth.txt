CHAPTER 12: January 9th

The first rehab hadn't taken because I never showed up to it. I was there — Laguna Beach, ocean nearby, the soap opera actor for a friend, the worksheets filled out in handwriting that looked like progress — but I wasn't there. I was checking boxes. I'd gone through the motions of surrender without surrendering anything, and you cannot fake that step. The disease knows the difference even when everyone around you can't. I'd walked out of Laguna still telling my therapist I couldn't live without the pills, and I'd been right, in the only way that mattered: the version of me that walked in was the version that walked out.

This time was different, and I can tell you exactly why. The first time, I went because a judge told me to. This time, I went because I had run out of every other direction. There is a kind of defeat that finally stops being a tragedy and starts being a door, and I had arrived at it. Lou had gotten me to the threshold. He'd carried me as far as a person can carry another person — which is all the way to the door and not one inch past it. The inch past it was mine.

Detox is not dignified. I'll spare the catalog, but I'll say this much so that anyone reading it from inside their own version knows I'm not cleaning it up: the body does not let go of what's been running it without a fight, and the fight happens in your skin and your stomach and the long hours of the night when there's nothing to do but feel every decibel of the static you'd spent a decade turning down. I had been using, in one form or another, to keep that static quiet since I was a kid panicking over which aunt wore which color nail polish. The pills had simply been the most effective version of a thing I'd been doing my whole life — reaching for the illusion of control, the volume knob, anything to keep the floor from coming up. Now there was no knob. There was just the noise, and me, and the slow, clinical, unglamorous work of letting the noise be there without answering it.

What I remember most clearly is the quiet that came after — not the absence of craving, the craving stayed, but the strange flat clearing on the far side of the worst of it. I was thirty years old. I had nothing. No job, no money, no driver's license, a hundred and thirteen thousand dollars of debt I hadn't yet had the courage to add up, a name on a wall in a New Jersey gym that belonged to a kid I no longer recognized. And underneath the wreckage, for the first time in longer than I could remember, no anesthesia. I could feel everything. That terrified me. It was also the first true thing I'd felt in years.

I started doing the things they tell you to do, the things that sound too simple to work. The folding chairs. The bad coffee. The same stories told by different people at different stages of ruin and repair. I listened more than I talked, and when I talked I didn't tie it up neatly. I just told the truth, which was a muscle I'd let go completely slack and had to rebuild one honest sentence at a time. I'd learn later that this is the whole thing — not the dramatic surrender but the repetition, the showing up to the same room to do the same un-dramatic work, day after day, until trust gets rebuilt not by talent or charisma but by the simple accumulation of days you didn't run.

There was one date that organized all of it, though I didn't know yet how completely.

January 9, 2009.

That's the day. I didn't choose it for its symmetry — the symmetry chose me, slowly, across the years that followed. I would not understand it standing there at thirty, newly hollowed out and afraid. I'd won the county championship at seventeen. Lou had been in the third row that day, the way he was always in the third row. And seventeen years after January 9, 2009 — to the day — a red cardinal would land on the mirror of my car in a nursing-home parking lot in Raleigh, fifteen days after I buried the man who'd driven the U-Haul, and the number would arrive complete: seventeen years sober, the age I'd been when I won, the foundation and the loss folded into a single morning. None of that had happened yet. On January 9, 2009, it was just a day I got sober, and I had no idea it would become the truest anniversary of my life.

Lou made it one. From the very first year, he made a production of it. He came to Gulf Breeze Hospital that first January to hear me speak — to sit in a room and listen to his son talk about staying sober for one entire year, as if it were the county championship all over again, as if one year of not dying were a title worth driving to watch. Every January 9th after that, he made the call, made the deal of it. He told me once, quietly, that there wasn't a time he said my name to someone who knew me that they didn't come back with the same thing: he's the best. That was what he was proud of. Not the commissions I used to chase. Not the screens my face had played on. The name. The reputation. The man who was finally being built behind it. He'd handed me his last twenty dollars when I was a kid and never mentioned it for years. Now he was handing me something bigger and managing, again, to make it feel like nothing he needed credit for: the unshakable expectation that the date would hold.

It held.

It has held every year since, and I have come to understand sobriety the way Jeff would later teach me to — not as a ceiling but as a floor. The day I got sober was not the finish line. It was the foundation. Everything in this book that came after — Hope, the Salmon Palace, the home health rooms, the hospice rooms, the families holding hands that are getting cooler, the man I am able to be when I sit in someone's worst day and don't flinch — all of it stands on January 9, 2009. On a thirty-year-old with nothing, in a folding chair, with no anesthesia left, finally doing the one thing his father could not do for him.

Lou got me to the door. He'd gotten Charlie to a door too, once, and lost him. He knew the odds. He drove the U-Haul anyway, bought the last glass of wine anyway, delivered me anyway, and then stood back — because the man who'd thrown me into a pool at six years old so I'd learn I could keep myself at the surface understood the deepest version of that lesson. He could put me at the edge of the water. He could not breathe for me.

I swam. Barely, at first. Arms moving the way bodies move when they're trying not to go under, before technique, before form, before anything except the instruction to stay at the surface.

But I swam. And then the next day I swam again.
