ME AND DAD

The Way It Should Have Been

Cover art: Dad and the boy at golden hour, mid-game, laughing — or a porch silhouette with two gloves on the rail.

Dedication
For Dad.

For the boy I was, still waiting by the window.

And for everyone learning that love can be built —
even out of what went missing.
✿
2
Art: Saturday-morning kitchen, sunlight. Dad with coffee in one hand, my glove held out in the other.

In the book of should-have-beens, you’re there on Saturday mornings — coffee in one hand, my glove in the other.

“Ready, kiddo?”

In that book, I’m always ready.

3

In that book, we play until the streetlights hum.

Soccer. Baseball. It never matters which.

In the book of should-have-beens, you never miss a game.

4
Art: Dad steadying the boy on a bike, or crouched behind him adjusting his grip on a bat.

You teach me the things dads teach: how to ride, how to throw, how to get back up.

Mostly how to get back up.

The turn

But that book isn’t the one we got.

Some pages stayed empty. Some chairs stayed empty, too. I waited a long time by that window.

“Look,” Mom whispered. “A cardinal.”
Even on the empty days, something red always came to the rail.

6
Art: the boy older now, notebook or glove in his lap, the cardinal near. Light beginning to warm.

Here’s what I finally learned, and it took me years:

an empty page isn’t the end of a story.

It’s an invitation to write.

7
Art: the grown man playing catch with a child at golden hour — the fist-bump composition rhymes perfectly here. Fully warm palette.

So I grew up and became the dad I was waiting for —

the one who shows up, who stays, who never misses a game.

Every should-have-been became an instruction:
this is what you do.

8
Author’s Note
This is the book I couldn’t read as a boy, because nobody wrote it. My dad and I didn’t get the Saturday mornings, the sidelines, the long games of catch. For a long time that felt like an ending.

It was Lou who showed me the other way to read an empty page — as a place where you get to write. Show up. Stay. Keep your word. I write those words now with my own hands, in my own family, and at bedsides where showing up is the whole gift.

If your book has empty pages too, this one is for you. Pick up the pen.

— Rob Brizzi
