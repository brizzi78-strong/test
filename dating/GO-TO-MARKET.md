# Cardinal — go-to-market playbook

Expert-style GTM advice for launching a **women-first, safety-first dating
app**. The product is built (see the demo); this is how you get the first
10,000 people to use it. The whole game is **liquidity** — enough of the right
people, in the same place, at the same time.

---

## 1. The only problem that matters at launch: cold start

A dating app with no one on it is worthless — the classic chicken-and-egg. You
do **not** solve this with ads. You solve it by **going absurdly narrow** and
building one dense, working network before you widen.

**The rule: pick one city, ideally one community inside it.** A dating app that
is "live in 50 cities" is dead in all 50. One that owns a single college town,
neighborhood, or scene is alive. Density beats reach — always, at the start.

Your women-first thesis makes this sharper: **seed the women, the men follow.**
So the launch is not "acquire users." It's "acquire *women* in one place, then
let the men in."

---

## 2. The launch playbook (0 → 1)

1. **Choose the beachhead.** One city with a dense, social, safety-conscious
   target: a university town, or a specific urban scene (grad students, a
   professional network, a faith or affinity community). The repo already leans
   NC — a Triangle-area (Raleigh/Durham/Chapel Hill) college + young-professional
   launch is a credible, defensible wedge.
2. **Recruit ~200–500 women first, by hand.** Founder-led, not paid. Campus
   ambassadors, group chats, sororities, run clubs, book clubs, alumni networks.
   Personal invites convert; ads don't at this stage.
3. **Only then open to men** — and let the waitlist/ratio do the marketing
   ("women are already here"). Consider a soft cap on the male:female ratio so
   the experience never tips.
4. **Make the first event the product.** Cardinal's monthly events aren't just
   retention — they're the acquisition engine. A great real-world mixer in one
   city creates word of mouth no ad can buy, and it's where the safety promise
   becomes tangible.
5. **Manufacture the "it's alive" moment.** First matches, first dates, first
   event photos → collect and amplify them. Early social proof is everything.

---

## 3. Seeding the hard side (women) — tactics

- **Founder-led, high-touch.** Personally onboard the first cohort. Ask each for
  3 friends. This is unscalable and correct.
- **Ambassadors / hosts.** Recruit well-connected women as paid/perked local
  hosts who run events and bring their networks.
- **Partner with the community, not the internet.** Yoga studios, run clubs,
  universities, women's professional groups, local businesses for event venues.
- **Lead with safety in the pitch to women** — background checks, verification,
  no unsolicited-message hell. That is the reason to switch, and it's the
  demographic that cares most.
- **Referral loop:** invite-a-friend gives status/perks (event priority), not
  discounts. Women invite women; that's the flywheel.

---

## 4. Positioning & message

- **One sentence:** *"The dating app where every member is background-checked,
  and women are actually safe."*
- **Category of one:** don't position against Tinder on volume — position on
  **safety, intention, and staying** ("cardinals mate for life"). You're the
  anti-swipe, pro-real-connection app.
- **Proof over adjectives:** "background-checked," "verified," "we meet in
  person monthly" are concrete and rare. Say those, not "meaningful
  connections."
- **The founder story is a channel.** Why this, why now, why safety — a
  credible founder narrative earns press and trust that ad spend can't.

---

## 5. Channels, roughly in priority order

1. **Founder-led / community outreach** (0→1): the only thing that works early.
2. **Events as marketing** (0→N): every event is content, PR, and referral fuel.
3. **Referral / invite** (once there's a working core).
4. **Organic social + content:** TikTok/IG built on real member stories,
   safety education, and event footage — not generic app ads.
5. **Campus ambassadors** if the beachhead is collegiate (Tinder's original
   playbook — launch at parties, seed both sides in one room).
6. **PR / earned media:** the safety + women-first angle is genuinely newsworthy
   in a market people love to criticize.
7. **Paid ads: last, and only to pour fuel on a fire that's already lit** in a
   proven city. Paid before liquidity just burns money.

---

## 6. Pricing GTM (tie the money to the strategy)

- **Free for women — permanently, and loudly.** It's positioning as much as
  pricing: it signals who the app is *for*.
- **Delay the men's paywall.** At launch, keep men mostly free to build
  liquidity; introduce Cardinal+ ($19.99/mo) once there's real value to gate
  (matches, "see who liked you"). Monetize the fire, don't smother the spark.
- **Charge for the outcome men want most:** access and reciprocity signals
  (who-liked-you), not core function. The demo already models this.
- **Watch the unit economics:** background-check cost per signup vs. Cardinal+
  conversion. If checks are gated to paid users, the math changes — model it
  before scaling spend.

---

## 7. Metrics that actually matter

- **Liquidity / ratio:** active men : active women per city. Guard it.
- **Match → conversation → date** conversion (the real funnel, not installs).
- **Time-to-first-match** for a new woman (should be fast, or she leaves).
- **D7 / D30 retention** by gender (women's retention is the leading indicator).
- **Event attendance & repeat rate** (your differentiated engagement loop).
- **CAC vs. LTV** — but ignore CAC until liquidity works; it's meaningless on an
  empty network.

Vanity metrics to ignore early: total downloads, total signups, app-store rank.

---

## 8. Scaling (1 → N)

- **Only expand a city once the current one is self-sustaining** (organic
  growth, healthy ratio, repeat events). Copy the *playbook* city by city — the
  same hand-seed-women-then-events motion — not a national ad blast.
- Each new city is a fresh cold start. Respect that; don't dilute.
- Build a repeatable "city launch kit": ambassador program, first-event
  template, local partnerships, seeding targets.

---

## 9. Pitfalls that kill dating apps

- **Launching everywhere at once** → dead everywhere. Go narrow.
- **Buying users before liquidity** → expensive churn.
- **Ratio collapse** (too many men) → the women leave, then everyone leaves.
  This is the #1 killer; protect the ratio ruthlessly.
- **Safety as marketing copy only** → if the real moderation/verification isn't
  there, one bad story ends the trust. The promise must be operationally true.
- **Monetizing too early** → smothers the network before it's alive.

---

## 10. Reference playbooks worth studying

- **Tinder** — launched by seeding both sides in one room (USC parties/Greek
  life): density in a single community first.
- **Bumble** — women-first as product *and* brand; campus ambassador army;
  safety/empowerment positioning. Closest analog to Cardinal — study it hard.
- **Hinge** — won on *intention* ("designed to be deleted") against swipe
  fatigue; proof that a differentiated, anti-Tinder position can break through.

---

*GTM is a sequence, not a budget. Win one city with the women-first + safety +
events motion, prove the loop and the ratio, then repeat. The product is ready;
the first city is the whole job.*
