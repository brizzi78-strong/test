# Line-edit sample — the opening

This shows how I'd line-edit *in your voice*. The headline is that the prose needs a
**light touch, not a rewrite.** Most of what follows is "leave it alone, here's why,"
with a few surgical cuts. If this is the level of intervention you want across the
book, say so and I'll do the full pass.

Convention: ~~strikethrough~~ = cut, **bold** = added, → notes explain the *why*.

## Foreword (excerpt)

The foreword is strong and personal where it tells Dave's own story. The one place I'd
cut is the paragraph that tells the reader how to receive the book:

> ~~You can trust what you are about to read. Rob does not have a long list of titles
> after his name, and he has never needed one. He has done the harder thing. He has
> lived it.~~

→ **Why:** The chapters earn the reader's trust through scene (the jail cell, the pool,
the nineteen-second pin). Telling the reader "you can trust this" up front does the
opposite of what the book's restraint does everywhere else. The flagpole story two
paragraphs earlier already *is* this point, dramatized. Cut the instruction; keep the
scene. (This is the §6 note from the editorial letter, shown concretely.)

Everything in the foreword that is *Dave's* — the flagpole, the 35-year friendship, the
reunion in tears — stays. That material only he can give.

## Prologue — "The Cardinal"

**Original:**

> Eleven thirty-seven in the morning. January gray.
>
> I sat in a facility parking lot with coffee going cold in my hand and looked up into
> the sky.
>
> I took a sip. Then I saw the cardinal on my driver's side mirror.

**Edited:**

> Eleven thirty-seven in the morning. January gray.
>
> I sat in a ~~facility~~ parking lot with coffee going cold in my hand ~~and looked up
> into the sky~~.
>
> I took a sip. Then I saw the cardinal on my driver's side mirror.

→ **Two small cuts, two reasons.**
> - **"facility"** is vague and faintly clinical on page one — whose facility? It also
>   pre-empts a reveal: Ch 37 tells us this is a parking lot in Raleigh on your
>   sobriety anniversary. Let the prologue be just "a parking lot" and let Ch 37 do the
>   work. (Continuity catch #4.)
> - **"and looked up into the sky"** quietly misdirects: the cardinal turns out to be
>   on the *side mirror* — down and to the left, not in the sky. Cutting the skyward
>   look makes the cardinal's appearance land where it actually is.

The rest of the prologue I would not touch:

> Close enough to see the texture of its feathers. Red against the dirty silver. It
> looked ahead like it had somewhere to be and decided, for a moment, not to go.
>
> My body went still.
>
> The tears came quietly. No sound at first. Just my vision blurring and my hands
> pressing into my thighs.
>
> The cardinal stayed for two minutes.
>
> It turned its head once. And was gone.

→ "Red against the dirty silver" and "decided, for a moment, not to go" are exactly the
book's register. Leave it.

## Chapter One — "Tijuana"

This chapter is the strongest argument for a light touch. It's nearly clean. I'd change
**one** thing and flag a second for your call.

> Then the hands. Around my throat.
>
> I rolled before I knew I was awake. Got a grip on his wrist. Pushed him off and took
> him down to his back, my elbow into his neck.
>
> The wrestler was still in there. Somewhere underneath everything else I had become.

→ Keep all of this. "The wrestler was still in there" is the spine of the whole book in
one line — it connects the jail cell to the basement mat 200 pages earlier.

**The one change:**

> His eyes were black. ~~No soul behind them.~~

→ **Why:** "No soul behind them" is the single place in the chapter where the spare
style relaxes into cliché. "His eyes were black" is stronger alone — it's concrete and
it lets the reader do the work, which is what the rest of the chapter trusts them to do.
If you want to keep a second beat, replace it with something physical (what the eyes
*did*) rather than what they lacked.

**Flag for your call** (not a cut):

> A homemade tattoo of a bird ran down the side of his face.

→ Keep — but notice what you've done, possibly by accident: there's a **bird on the
face of the man choking you** in chapter one, and a **cardinal** that frames the book.
A violent bird and a merciful one. If that resonance is intentional, it's working
quietly and you should leave it exactly this quiet. If it wasn't intentional — now it
is, and you still leave it alone.

The ending of the chapter needs nothing:

> I went home. I showered. I went to work.
>
> The next day I drove back to Mexico.

→ This is the best kind of ending the book does. The flat declaratives and the white
space carry the whole weight of the addiction the editorial letter says is otherwise
off-page. *Don't add a sentence here.* The restraint is the point.

## What this sample is telling you

Two cuts in the prologue, one in chapter one, one paragraph trimmed from the foreword.
That's the right intensity for prose this controlled. The work this book needs is mostly
**structural** (the editorial letter) — where chapters go, how much of the addiction
gets rendered, closing the cardinal frame — not line-level. If you want, I'll run this
same light pass across all 41 chapters and deliver it as tracked changes you can accept
or reject one at a time.
