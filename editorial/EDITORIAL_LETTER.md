# Editorial Letter — *The Cardinal's Promise*

*Working manuscript, Foreword + Prologue + Chapters 1–41. ~20,000 words.*

---

## The short version

This is good. The voice is the asset: spare, declarative, heavy white space, the
`. . .` beats, the refrains (*That was Lou. / I had a life. / Watch first. Then
go.*), and the cardinal framing the whole book. My job here is to protect that
voice, not sand it down. So almost everything below is either **structural** (where
chapters go, what's missing, what's scaffolding) or **continuity** (names, dates,
ages that don't line up). The line-level work is light on purpose — see
`line-edit_opening-sample.md` for what a faithful pass actually looks like.

The decisions only you can make are flagged **[AUTHOR DECISION]**. Everything else
I can execute on your say-so.

## What's working — keep your hands off it

- **The motif discipline.** The cardinal (Prologue → Ch 37), the water ("the pool
  taught me my body could keep me up; the mat taught me I could decide to walk into
  the water"), *two hands*, *watch first*, the door left open a crack, the
  ice-cream surprises that rhyme across 24 years (Cooperstown → Cold Stone), "rooms
  Lou walked into first," and the **"We?"** dedication paying off in Ch 39. These
  are doing real structural work. Don't touch them.
- **Restraint.** You trust the reader. Bryan Farrell going back for another slice
  of pizza after pinning you in nineteen seconds does more than a paragraph of
  feeling would. The book is full of this.
- **The present-tense childhood / past-tense adulthood split.** Childhood as an
  eternal present, recovery as something completed and looked back on. That's a
  real choice and it lands.

## The big structural questions

### 1. Where is the addiction? **[AUTHOR DECISION]**
The subtitle is *a boy from Mexico, an addict, a son*. But the addiction is almost
entirely **off-page** — it exists as fragments and references: the Tijuana cell
(Ch 1), "hit-and-ran a Del Taco," "Camp Pendleton," "West Palm Beach," "the Edge,"
"First Run in Oxford," "the condo. The bottle. The cell." Each fragment is
powerful, but the reader never gets the *descent* or the *turn* as rendered scene.
Right now addiction is connective tissue holding up a father book.

You have to decide which book this is:
- **A father book that touches addiction**, in which case the fragments are fine and
  we just tune them, or
- **An addiction-and-recovery book held up by a father**, which is what the subtitle
  promises — in which case 3–5 chapters need to *render* the descent and the turn
  (the West Palm Beach morning Lou is at the door; the drive; January 9, 2009).
  Your most dramatic material is currently in summary.

My recommendation: the subtitle and the framing device (you open in a jail cell)
are writing a check the middle of the book doesn't yet cash. Lean toward the second
book.

### 2. Chronology and the interleave
The childhood thread is roughly linear; the present-day/recovery thread is
interleaved. Mostly it works, but the jumps can disorient — Ch 33 leaps to "thirty-
two years old," then 35–36 drop back to teenage wrestling, then 37 jumps to "twelve
years sober." A few present-day chapters anchor themselves with a date up top
(38, 39, 40, 41) and it helps enormously; others don't. **Recommendation:** map
every chapter to (a) narrative age and (b) the sobriety clock, decide the interleave
deliberately, and give the present-day chapters a light dateline/age anchor the way
38–41 already do.

### 3. Ch 34 ("The Call") is already flagged as misplaced
You've bracketed it yourself: *"Chronologically this chapter belongs in the freshman
year section, around age 14… To be moved during revision."* Agreed — it belongs back
in the teenage section. I can move it whenever you want.

### 4. The "Raw Material for Tomorrow" block is scaffolding inside the book
The "My Brother's Keeper" notes (fake license, Miami arrest, the ultimatum, India /
Bell's palsy, the bachelor party) are sitting in the manuscript body between Ch 34
and Ch 35. That's a note-to-self a reader should never see — pull it into a separate
notes file. Two real observations about it: (a) **Chris is underdeveloped** relative
to how much he matters, and this material is the fix; (b) the **ultimatum** ("Admit
you have a problem or admit you have mental health issues. Pick one.") is one of the
most important beats in the whole recovery arc and right now it only exists as a
bullet point.

### 5. The ending isn't the ending **[AUTHOR DECISION]**
The draft ends at the 2019 wedding (Ch 41) — a genuine emotional peak; the "fist
quietly raised… not credit, not victory, completion" beat is excellent. But the book
*opens* on the cardinal and (via Ch 37) on Lou's death Christmas morning 2025. That
frame is currently open. The book will almost certainly want to close it — to land
back on the death and the cardinal it began with. The manuscript is marked "end of
current draft," so I assume you know this; flagging it so the final movement gets
planned rather than drifted into.

### 6. The Foreword previews too much **[AUTHOR DECISION]**
Dave Meyer's foreword is warm and the flagpole story is great. But it paraphrases the
book's own thesis — the flagpole = "getting back up," wrestling = the internal
contest, the addiction credential, hospice as "the last chapter," Lou and the fly
balls — and then tells the reader how to feel ("You can trust what you are about to
read"). The chapters deliver all of that more powerfully through scene. Consider
trimming it toward Dave's *personal vantage* (the 35-year friendship, the flagpole,
the reunion that left you both in tears) and away from summarizing your virtues and
the book's argument. See the sample file for a specific suggested cut.

## Continuity & fact catches (concrete — these are the ones an author wants caught)

1. **Age math in 1986 (Ch 32, "Mag").** The chapter says *"It is 1986… I am five
   years old."* But every other anchor puts your birth year ≈ **1978**: age 32 on
   Jan 9 2010 (Ch 33); "thirty-eight years old" in March 2017 (Ch 39); "forty-seven
   years old now," writing 2026 (Ch 30); 17th sobriety anniversary Jan 9 2026, i.e.
   sober since 2009 (Ch 37). In 1986 you'd be **~7–8, not 5.** Either the year or the
   age needs to move. *(Same scene: Chris with a Game Boy in 1986 — the Game Boy
   launched 1989. Small anachronism in the same paragraph.)*

2. **Coach: "Jonesy" vs "Ronnie."** Ch 35 names the wrestling coach **Jonesy**
   throughout. Ch 36 ("Robby, No") refers to the same man — three years your coach,
   Pinball Wizard, knew every opponent's strengths — as **Ronnie**. The reader is
   never told they're the same person. If it's nickname-vs-given-name, bridge it once
   on the page; otherwise standardize to one.

3. **"Quint" is named before he's introduced.** Ch 33: the man at your one-year
   anniversary arrives anonymously ("A man comes up to me afterward…"), then is
   referred to as **Quint** a few lines later as if we already know him. Give him his
   name in the scene ("He told me his name was Quint") so the later references —
   including "Quint at Chumuckla" in Ch 37 — read cleanly.

4. **Prologue vs Ch 37 location.** Same cardinal event. Prologue: *"a facility
   parking lot."* Ch 37: *"a parking lot in Raleigh, North Carolina,"* Jan 9 2026.
   The payoff is intentional and good. Just harmonize the wording — "facility parking
   lot" is vague and faintly clinical on page one (see sample).

5. **Becky / Mama.** Mother is "Mama" in childhood, "Becky" from ~Ch 30 on; Ch 41
   does the handoff well ("Then my mother stood up. Becky."). Confirm the switch
   point is deliberate. (Lou calling her "Becky" in dialogue early, Ch 12, is fine.)

6. **Density of the signature devices.** "I do not say anything" / "He did not say
   anything," and the anaphora ("I had a wife. I had a brother…"), are deliberate and
   mostly land. They cluster densest in **Ch 28** (the "I do not say anything" beat
   four times in a short span) and **Ch 14** (the long "I know…" run). The device
   stays powerful by being rationed — recommend thinning the two or three densest
   clusters so it never tips toward self-parody.

7. **Within-chapter tense shifts.** Mostly intentional and effective (the memory
   "completing" into past tense — e.g., Ch 13 shifts from present to "Mama drove us to
   the airport"). A couple read as accidental rather than as a beat. Worth a
   dedicated pass to confirm each shift is doing work.

8. **Minor factual:** Ch 32 baseball (Mattingly grounds out; Righetti in the ninth;
   Cal Ripken Jr. two-run homer to lose it). Specific enough to be worth checking
   against a real 1986 Yankees–Orioles box score if precision matters to you.
