# The Ultimate Book Marketing Plan

> **Read [BOOK_SALES_THESIS.md](BOOK_SALES_THESIS.md) first.** It is the evidence-based
> argument — built from the documented success of prior top-selling nonfiction — for
> *why* this plan is sequenced the way it is, and it defines the measurable launch
> gates (G1–G5) that decide when this plan is ready to execute. For nonfiction, the
> thesis re-weights this plan: platform-building and the earned-media tour lead;
> ads and retail mechanics support.

A complete, execution-ready plan to maximize book sales. Work it in order: positioning
first, assets second, launch third, then the long game. Most books fail at step 1 and
try to fix it with ads at step 4 — money spent on marketing a badly positioned book is
money spent finding out the positioning is bad.

## Decision Summary

| Decision | Call | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| Primary sales engine | **Amazon (KDP)** first, wide later | 60–80% of ebook sales happen there; win one store before splitting effort |
| Exclusivity | **KDP Select for launch (90 days)**, then reassess | Kindle Unlimited page reads are a second income stream and boost rank; easy to go wide after |
| Launch price | **$0.99 ebook for launch week, then $4.99** | Low price maximizes launch-week velocity (rank + reviews); $4.99 is the ebook profit sweet spot at 70% royalty |
| Review strategy | **ARC team of 20–50 readers before launch** | 10+ reviews in week one is the difference between the algorithm helping you and ignoring you |
| Email list | **Start immediately, before launch** | The only marketing channel you own; every other channel rents you access to your readers |
| Paid ads | **After 10+ reviews and a converting product page** — not before | Ads amplify what exists; they can't fix a page that doesn't convert |
| Budget floor | Plan works at **$0**; scales at $500 and $2,500 tiers | Most levers (positioning, email, ARCs, launch sequencing) cost time, not money |

## 1. Positioning — do this before anything else

Everything downstream (cover, blurb, keywords, ads) is an expression of positioning.
Get these four answers in writing:

1. **Who is the reader?** Not "everyone who likes `<GENRE>`" — the specific person:
   what they just finished reading, what they want to feel or learn, where they hang out.
2. **Comparable titles ("comps").** 3–5 books published in the last ~3 years that the
   ideal reader has already bought. These drive category choice, cover direction, ad
   targeting, and the "for fans of X" line.
3. **The hook.** One sentence that makes the target reader say "I need that." Test it:
   say it to 5 people in the target audience; if nobody asks a follow-up question, it's
   not a hook yet.
4. **The promise.** What the reader gets — transformation (nonfiction) or emotional
   experience (fiction). The blurb, cover, and ads all make this same promise.

**Deliverable:** a one-page positioning doc: reader profile, 5 comps, hook, promise.

## 2. The Product Page — your real storefront

The Amazon product page converts browsers into buyers. Optimize each element in
order of impact:

### Cover
- Must look at home next to the comps **at thumbnail size** — buyers see it at
  ~100px first. Clarity beats cleverness.
- Genre signaling is the whole job. A beautiful cover in the wrong genre style sells
  nothing. Hire a designer who works in `<GENRE>` specifically (check their portfolio
  for books ranking in your categories).
- Budget: $300–$800 for a pro cover; premade genre covers run $100–$300 and outsell
  most DIY.

### Blurb (book description)
- **First line is 80% of the work** — it shows above the fold. Lead with the hook,
  never with backstory or "In a world…".
- Structure: hook → stakes/promise → 2–3 short paragraphs → call to action
  ("Buy now to…" / "Perfect for fans of `<COMP AUTHOR>`").
- Format with bold, line breaks, short paragraphs. Walls of text kill conversion.

### Keywords and categories
- **7 KDP keyword slots:** use multi-word phrases readers actually type
  ("enemies to lovers fantasy romance", not "fantasy"). Mine them from the Amazon
  search bar autocomplete and from comp titles' also-boughts.
- **Categories:** you get 3 at setup. Pick the most specific niche
  categories where the comps rank and where the #1 book's rank is beatable
  (check its Best Sellers Rank; a category whose #1 sits at 50,000 BSR is winnable,
  one whose #1 is at 500 is not — yet).
- "Bestseller in a niche category" is an orange badge on your page. Badges convert.

### Look Inside / sample
- The first 10% sells the other 90%. Front-load story or value; move dedications,
  acknowledgments, and long prefaces to the back.

### Author page
- Complete the Amazon Author Central profile: photo, bio written for the reader
  (what they get, not your CV), links. Claim the book.

## 3. Pricing & Distribution

- **Ebook:** $0.99 launch week → $4.99 standard. $2.99–$9.99 is the 70%-royalty band
  on KDP; $4.99 usually maximizes revenue-per-sale × conversion for a full-length book.
- **Paperback:** price for a $2–$4 royalty after printing costs; the paperback price
  also makes the ebook look cheap by comparison (anchoring).
- **Kindle Unlimited (KDP Select):** enroll for the first 90 days. Page reads pay
  royalties, KU readers take chances on new authors, and Select unlocks Countdown
  Deals / Free days for later promos. Reassess at day 90: if KU reads are a big share
  of income, stay; if not, go wide (Kobo, Apple, B&N, library channels via Draft2Digital).
- **Audiobook:** biggest growth format. Plan it for month 2–6, not launch — it's a
  second launch event and a second product page.

## 4. The Email List — the asset that compounds

Start before launch, even with zero subscribers.

- **Reader magnet:** a free short story, prequel chapter, bonus epilogue (fiction) or
  checklist/template/companion resource (nonfiction), delivered for an email signup.
- **Signup placement:** front and back matter of the book ("Get the free `<MAGNET>` at
  `<URL>`"), author website, social bios. Back matter converts best — those readers
  just finished and liked the book.
- **Cadence:** one email every 2–4 weeks. Talk like a person, not a brand. Share
  progress, recommendations, behind-the-scenes. The list exists so that launch day
  is an announcement to warm readers, not a cold start.
- **Tooling:** any mainstream provider works; automation to set up on day one:
  welcome sequence (3 emails: deliver magnet → who you are → your book).
- **Growth accelerators:** newsletter swaps with authors in your genre, group
  promos (e.g. BookFunnel/StoryOrigin bundles), the back of every book you ever publish.

## 5. Reviews — social proof is the gate

Amazon's algorithm and human buyers both key on review count and recency.

- **ARC (Advance Review Copy) team:** recruit 20–50 readers pre-launch — from the
  email list, genre Facebook groups, StoryOrigin/BookFunnel/BookSirens ARC programs.
  Send the book 2–4 weeks early with a launch-day reminder to post an honest review.
- **Target: 10+ reviews in week one, 50+ by month three.** Ad platforms
  informally gate on this too (BookBub Featured Deals want dozens).
- **Back-matter ask:** a single polite page — "Reviews are the lifeblood of new
  authors. If you enjoyed this, two sentences on Amazon makes a real difference."
- **Never:** buy reviews, swap reviews with other authors on-platform, review your
  own book via alts, or have close family review from the same household/IP. Amazon
  bans for all of these, and the ban is for life.
- **Editorial reviews** (nonfiction especially): Kirkus/BookLife/genre blogs give
  quotable lines for the blurb's "Editorial Reviews" section.

## 6. Pre-Launch Timeline

### 90–60 days out
- [ ] Positioning doc finalized (reader, comps, hook, promise)
- [ ] Cover commissioned from a genre-specific designer
- [ ] Reader magnet written; email list + welcome automation live
- [ ] Author website (one page is enough: book, bio, signup) and social profiles claimed
- [ ] Start showing up where the readers are (2 platforms max — pick the ones the
      target reader actually uses; for most genres today that's TikTok/Instagram
      for fiction, X/LinkedIn/YouTube for nonfiction)

### 60–30 days out
- [ ] Blurb drafted and tested (read by 5 target readers; revise on their questions)
- [ ] Keywords + categories researched and chosen
- [ ] ARC team recruited (20–50 readers); ARCs distributed
- [ ] Amazon pre-order live (optional — pre-orders spread sales out rather than
      spiking launch day; skip for book 1 unless building a series pipeline)
- [ ] Pitch podcasts/blogs/newsletters in the niche (6–8 week lead times are normal)

### 30–7 days out
- [ ] Product page finalized: cover, blurb, Look Inside checked on desktop + mobile
- [ ] Launch-week promo stack booked (see §7 — promo sites need 1–4 weeks lead)
- [ ] Launch announcement email drafted; social posts drafted and scheduled
- [ ] ARC reminder sent: "review goes live on launch day, here's the link"
- [ ] Author Central profile complete

### Launch week (the 5-day push)
- [ ] Price at $0.99; announce to email list on day 1 (this is the single
      highest-converting send you will ever do)
- [ ] Promo-site features run mid-week (stacked, see §7)
- [ ] Daily social presence — behind-the-scenes, reader reactions, milestone posts
      ("#1 in `<CATEGORY>`!" with a screenshot), not "buy my book" on repeat
- [ ] Personally thank every reviewer/sharer you can
- [ ] Day 5–7: raise price to $4.99, announce "last chance at $0.99" the day before
      (urgency converts; it's also simply true)

## 7. The Promo Stack — buying velocity

Paid newsletter features put the deal-priced book in front of large deal-seeking
audiences. Book them so they land on consecutive launch-week days — the goal is
sustained rank velocity, which triggers Amazon's own recommendation machine
(also-boughts, "customers also read," category rank).

| Tier | Services (examples) | Cost each | Expect |
|---|---|---|---|
| Anchor | **BookBub Featured Deal** | $100–$1,000+ (category-dependent) | The big one; apply even though acceptance is <20% — reapply monthly |
| Mid | Freebooksy/Bargain Booksy, ENT, Robin Reads, Fussy Librarian | $25–$150 | Each reliably moves dozens–hundreds of $0.99 sales in-genre |
| Filler | Book Cave, Book Raid, genre newsletters | $5–$40 | Round out the stack on quiet days |

Re-run a promo stack every 60–90 days with a Kindle Countdown Deal — backlist
promotion is the cheapest sales you'll ever buy.

## 8. Paid Ads — the amplifier (month 2+)

Prerequisites: 10+ reviews, a product page that converts, and (ideally) more books —
ads on a one-book catalog rarely pay for themselves directly; they pay via
read-through on books 2, 3, 4.

- **Amazon Ads (start here):** Sponsored Products targeting comp titles and comp
  authors by ASIN/keyword. Start at $5–$10/day, auto + manual campaigns, cull
  keywords with clicks-but-no-sales weekly. Break-even ACOS for a $4.99 ebook at
  70% royalty is ~70%; treat anything under that as scaling fuel.
- **Facebook/Instagram ads:** better for free/0.99 promos and series starters;
  target comp-author interest audiences; creative = cover + hook line. Test at
  $10/day; kill anything above ~$0.50/click for deal traffic.
- **BookBub Ads (self-serve):** precise author-follower targeting; good for
  price-promo days. CPMs are high — use for spikes, not always-on.
- **Discipline:** one platform at a time, one variable per test, weekly review,
  and a spreadsheet of spend vs. royalties. Ads without tracking is donation.

## 9. The Long Game — what actually maximizes sales

The uncomfortable truth of book marketing: **the best marketing for a book is the
next book.** Repeat across every successful indie career:

1. **Write in a series or a tight niche.** Read-through turns one sale into four.
2. **Back matter chains:** every book ends with the next book's first chapter and a
   buy link, plus the newsletter signup.
3. **Relaunch moments:** new formats (audio, paperback, hardcover), box sets,
   anniversary promos, "book 2 launch" lifting book 1 — each is a fresh promo cycle.
4. **Email list compounding:** 100 subscribers at book 1 becomes 5,000 by book 4,
   and launch week stops being a gamble.
5. **Perennial content** (nonfiction): SEO articles, YouTube videos, podcast guesting
   that answer the questions the book answers — each is a permanent funnel to the
   product page.

## 10. Metrics — what to watch weekly

| Metric | Where | Healthy signal |
|---|---|---|
| Sales + KU page reads | KDP dashboard | Trend over 30 days, not daily noise |
| Best Sellers Rank | Product page | Sustained < 100k BSR ≈ selling daily |
| Review count + rating | Product page | +5–10/month early; ≥ 4.2 average |
| Email list size + open rate | Email provider | Any growth; > 40% opens |
| Ad spend vs. royalty | Ads console + KDP | ACOS < 70% ($4.99 book), or read-through-adjusted |
| Also-boughts | Product page | Comps appearing = algorithm has found your audience |

Review monthly; change **one** lever at a time or you won't know what worked.

The $0 column is slower, not weaker — positioning, ARC reviews, email, and launch
sequencing are the levers that matter, and they cost time.

## What This Plan Deliberately Avoids

- **Buying followers or reviews** — bans and dead audiences.
- **Being on every social platform** — two done well beats six done badly.
- **Ads before the page converts** — amplifying a leaky funnel.
- **Blast-marketing to non-readers** — press releases, generic "book promotion"
  services, and fiverr "we'll tweet your book to 100k followers" gigs sell to
  authors, not readers.
- **Waiting for the book to be discovered** — the algorithm rewards velocity you
  create; it doesn't create it for you.
